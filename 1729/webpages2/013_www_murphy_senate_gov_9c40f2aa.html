Implementing Smartphone Bans in K-12 Schools Offers Significant Benefits for Student Health

Date: October 02, 2024

While potentially controversial, California's recent legislation that instructs school districts to restrict student mobile phone usage during instructional time represents a major victory for the public health of children and teenagers, marking a significant move toward enhancing the mental wellness of students across the nation.

Available data clearly indicates that adolescents dedicate 40 hours weekly to their screens. This duration of screen time surged amid the COVID-19 pandemic, a period when electronic devices served as indispensable educational instruments for students in isolation.

Nevertheless, following the return to in-person learning, screen time has not decreased, and K-12 educational institutions are still observing persistent "achievement gaps" in mathematics and reading, which are disparities that have not improved since the pandemic.

Removing the distraction that phones present in the classroom is a necessary step to support student learning.

Almost all students in middle and high school acknowledge using their personal devices during school time, an issue that a majority of high school educators identify as a "major problem." Implementing restrictions on these devices throughout the school day provides a method for states to assist teachers and administrators in maintaining students' social and academic focus and engagement.

California has recently joined an increasing list of states that are implementing measures to tackle this issue. Policies regarding cell phones in schools are becoming more common; by June, a minimum of 15 states had enacted bans or limitations on student cell phone usage within school hours. Additional states have the opportunity to emulate this approach by adopting comparable measures, thereby aiding students in concentrating on their schoolwork instead of their phones during school hours.

Adolescents are dedicating more than five hours daily to social media, a habit that Surgeon General Vivek Murthy cautions may negatively impact their mental health.

He states, "Teenagers who use social media for over three hours per day have a twofold increased risk of experiencing symptoms of anxiety and depression," and he further notes that "almost 50 percent of adolescents report that social media contributes to negative feelings about their physical appearance."

As a component of his initiative to address loneliness, which he terms a public health "crisis," Murthy is advocating for the placement of warning labels on social media platforms. Studies indicate that addiction to cell phones can intensify feelings of loneliness; therefore, restricting social media access, even temporarily, has the potential to enhance the mental well-being of adolescents and foster more robust connections among students.

Evidence suggests that implementing these restrictions can result in favorable outcomes for students. Several years ago, Norway restricted the use of cell phones across hundreds of its middle schools, which led to a decrease in bullying incidents for both male and female students by more than 40 percent. The number of student appointments with psychology specialists fell by a remarkable 60 percent, while at the same time, their overall grade point averages showed an increase.

It is unsurprising that students frequently exhibit an automatic opposition to these suggested restrictions, given their familiarity with having constant access to their devices. However, educational institutions in the United States that have previously put phone restrictions in place have observed promising academic and behavioral improvements over a brief duration. One high school located in the Bronx, after establishing a ban on cell phones, witnessed a rise in its Advanced Placement test scores and an enhancement in overall academic achievement within a single year. Additionally, there was an increase in attendance at sporting events held after school hours. A number of students went so far as to express their gratitude to the school's principal for the policy, stating it enabled them to concentrate better during the school day.

In a different high school situated in upstate New York, there was a noticeable improvement in classroom participation, alongside a surge in student interaction, which was demonstrated by a more audible cafeteria since students were unable to quietly sit and browse their phones.

The principal of the school remarked, "Inside our classrooms, when you directly addressed the students, you received their full attention."

Specialists agree that prohibiting cell phones within schools can significantly contribute to the betterment of students' mental health.

"Children are experiencing feelings of isolation, loneliness, and disconnection, while also underperforming academically," states Zach Rausch, the principal researcher for the book, "The Anxious Generation."

"A significant cause for both of these issues is the integration of addictive technology into schools."

During this past summer, Senators Chris Murphy (D-Conn.) and Mitt Romney (R-Utah) put forward the Smart Kids Not Smartphones Act, a bill designed to acknowledge schools that enforce restrictions on cell phones in the classroom.

When introducing the legislation, Murphy highlighted that Connecticut's initiatives to limit cell phone use in schools have had a beneficial effect on mental health, echoing the comparable outcomes observed in other states.

Restricting mobile phones in K-12 schools nationwide has the potential to enhance adolescent health, promote student education, and foster stronger school environments. The United States would greatly benefit from a substantial measure of all these improvements.
