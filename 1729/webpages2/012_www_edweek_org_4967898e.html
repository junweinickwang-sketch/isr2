An Inside Look at Schools After Implementing Cellphone Bans

By Olina Banerji — February 04, 2025 | Corrected: February 05, 2025 | 7 min read

Correction Notice: An earlier version of this article contained an error regarding the location of Middleburgh Jr./Sr. High School. The school is situated in New York.

At the North Adams school district in Massachusetts, Timothy Callahan recognized that his colleagues were apprehensive about instituting a ban on cellphone use in their schools. Yet Callahan, serving as an assistant superintendent for the district, was certain that a properly executed prohibition would yield a "reasonable" enhancement in student discipline.

However, the actual data revealed outcomes that even Callahan had not anticipated.

He mentioned, "Achieving a 10 percent decrease would have been considered a success." "We observed a 75 percent decline in disciplinary referrals during the final quarter of the 2024 school year when contrasted with the equivalent quarter from the year prior. This change is almost entirely credited to the cellphone prohibition and the implementation of pouches," which secure student devices throughout the school day.

Callahan also noted that problems like students missing classes or detentions, or being outside of classrooms without authorization, have diminished "massively." Similarly, there has been a significant decrease in safety infractions, including physical horseplay and verbal disputes that could escalate into physical confrontations.

This positive shift in behavior is attributed by Callahan to students dedicating considerably less time to their phones, which they previously used for coordinating to skip class or gather in the hallways. He added that students are now demonstrating fewer distractions and are more attentive to their teachers' instructions.

"Educators are reporting an accelerated pace in covering their curriculum, which allows for the integration of more extensive, long-term assignments into their lesson plans," Callahan stated.

Across the country, a broad consensus among educators and mental health professionals identifies cellphones as a significant risk to the mental well-being and safety of students. The issue has become so pronounced that some teachers have chosen to leave the education field due to students' excessive dependence on these devices. Furthermore, unrestricted access to social media and new technologies, such as AI-generated "deepfakes," is creating apprehension about the potential for students to misuse these tools against fellow students and school staff.

These apprehensions have been duly noted by authorities. An analysis by Education Week reveals that no fewer than 19 states have enacted legislation or policies that either prohibit or limit student cellphone use in schools across the state, or advise local districts to establish their own such restrictions.

Approximately six district and school administrators have informed Education Week that the preliminary data on student discipline, collected after these prohibitions were established, shows promising results. These administrators have documented decreases in cellphone-related violations (students using phones during class), incidents of verbal and physical hostility, and various other conduct problems.

A casual survey of school administrators featured in Education Week’s Savvy Principal newsletter showed that 62 percent perceive an enhancement in student conduct following their school's implementation of cellphone limitations.

A significant number of leaders reported that their students, though reluctant, concede that they experience fewer distractions and are more involved in their lessons when cellphones are not present.

"Students are becoming less agitated over various issues," Callahan observed. "Their preoccupation with the activities of their peers has diminished. Moreover, they have expressed feeling a reduced sense of being targeted or attacked compared to when they carried cellphones with them."

Parents have also become supportive of the new policies. It is common for parents to initially resist cellphone restrictions in educational settings, frequently mentioning concerns about safety. The administrators who contributed to this article encountered comparable worries but successfully persuaded parents that the absence of cellphones would create a "distraction-free environment" conducive to student success. Presenting parents with the favorable data regarding discipline and student engagement has proven to be beneficial.

Fewer Disciplinary Problems Are Seen by Educators After Cellphone Bans Are Implemented

Improved behavior both inside and outside the classroom can serve as a catalyst for other positive developments.

In 2023, the Bentonville district in Arkansas initiated a pilot program for a cellphone ban at one of its high schools. Superintendent Debbie Jones reported that at the conclusion of the pilot, a teacher survey revealed that 86 percent noticed an improvement in student engagement from the 2022-23 to the 2023-24 school year, 75 percent observed increased classroom socialization, and there was a 57 percent decrease in verbal and physical aggression offenses.

Jones also highlighted a 51 percent reduction in drug-related incidents, which she attributes directly to the cellphone ban. She explained that students had previously used their phones to arrange drug exchanges.

"Nearly every disciplinary issue originates with a phone," she stated.

Following the pilot program's success, Jones implemented a district-wide policy prohibiting cellphones in all schools. For students from kindergarten through 8th grade, taking out cellphones during the school day is forbidden; for those in grades 9 through 12, phones must be turned off and put away. If a student is found using their phone during instructional time, the device is confiscated.

David Moore, the superintendent of Florida's Indian River County district, noted that the most substantial effect of a district-wide cellphone ban has been observed in his middle schools. Moore introduced the ban—which forbids elementary and middle school students from any phone use and high school students from using them during class—at the start of the 2023-24 academic year.

"The most significant issue with cellphone use was in our middle schools," Moore said. "We saw a 14 percent reduction in physical altercations at this point in the year compared to the same time last year."

Moore stated that prior to the cellphone ban, students engaged in cyberbullying, took unauthorized photos of teachers, and manipulated these images by placing teachers' heads onto different bodies. He confirmed that all such activities within the school's premises have ceased.

During the first year of the ban, 42 students were transferred to an alternative school for failing to adhere to the district's cellphone policies after receiving at least four warnings and other disciplinary actions. Moore reported that, thus far in the current school year, no students have been sent to the alternative school for this reason.

This particular statistic, according to Moore, illustrates "the severity of the issue, where certain students were so dependent on their phones that they continued to use them despite the consequences."

Two years ago, Middleburgh Jr./Sr. High School in New York put its cellphone restrictions into practice. Principal Matthew Sloane recounted that he initially encountered resistance from some high-achieving students who felt unfairly penalized by the new cellphone prohibition.

"I posed a question to those very students: 'How does it make you feel when a teacher must interrupt your lesson to instruct your peers to put their phones away?'" Sloane explained. This line of reasoning proved effective with the students.

Beyond a decrease in cyberbullying and other disciplinary problems at school, Sloane observed another change: as students became more attentive in class, teachers found it necessary to revise their lessons to be more engaging. He noted that students now voice their boredom and request more interactive lesson plans.

Cellphone Bans Represent a Leap of Faith That Educators Believe Is Worth Taking

The most effective method for ensuring a ban or policy is sustained is to enforce it across the entire district with clearly defined consequences, according to Jones of the Bentonville, Ark., district. She emphasized that the responsibility for implementing cellphone policies cannot be delegated to individual schools or teachers.

Bentonville has established a rigorous code of conduct regarding cellphones: upon arriving at school, students are required to either place their cellphones in Yondr pouches or keep them silenced inside their bags.

Should a student be found in violation of this policy, a structured set of consequences is initiated, escalating from phone confiscation to an in-school suspension. Jones asserted that educators must rigorously follow this plan to ensure students are fully aware of the repercussions for repeatedly using their cellphones.

"Nearly every disciplinary issue originates with a phone."
— Debbie Jones, superintendent of the Bentonville, Ark., school district

Without a uniform district-wide policy, managing cellphone use becomes a significant challenge for teachers. For instance, the North Adams district initially only required students to store their phones during class time, allowing usage in hallways or during lunch. Callahan noted that this approach frequently resulted in a "power struggle" between educators and students.

Some teachers enforced the rule more strictly, demanding students surrender their phones, while others were more permissive, leading to an inconsistent deterrent. Callahan mentioned that the district also attempted an "intermediate" solution by providing each teacher with a set of classroom lockers. This allowed them to confiscate a student's phone for the remainder of the period if they were caught using it.

"We likely invested around $5,000 in them. Ultimately, they had no discernible impact," Callahan remarked. The burden of enforcement still fell on individual teachers.

Under the new regulations, teachers can now simply notify a dean or the principal if they see a student with a cellphone. The device is then confiscated until the school day ends.

If a student does not comply, they are removed from the classroom. For a second offense, parents are required to sign a document to retrieve the phone.

Callahan reported that since the policy of no cellphones during school hours was enacted, infractions have slightly increased to four or five per day, but he anticipates this number will decrease as students adapt to the new regulations.

School leaders are hopeful that these initial improvements in student behavior will be lasting. Some are eager to see students capitalize on the newfound "freedom" from constant texting and social media engagement.

Jones expressed that cellphones in schools fostered an excessive reliance on parents, which hindered students' ability to think independently and solve problems for themselves.

"Did you forget your lunch? In that case, go and ask someone what options are available," she suggested. "It is crucial for them to develop the capacity to solve problems."
