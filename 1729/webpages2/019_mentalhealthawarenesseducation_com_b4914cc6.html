The Rationale for Implementing Cell Phone-Free Policies in Schools

Authored by AnneMoss Rogers | Published on October 28, 2024 | Categories: For Educators, For Parents, Mental Health, Student Wellness | 87 Comments

Defining a 'Ban' on Smartphones in an Educational Context

The question of whether cellphones should be permitted in classrooms requires clarification. To be precise, the discussion centers on smartphones, which are devices that connect to the internet and feature social media apps, as these represent the primary threat to student development and learning.

Considering the volume of data indicating that smartphone and social media use negatively affects youth¹, it becomes evident that schools need to be smartphone-free environments during the school day if we are to prioritize our children's education.²

Psychologist Jonathan Haidt, the author of The Anxious Generation, posits that children between 9 and 15 years old are especially vulnerable to the damaging effects of social media.³

Furthermore, the majority of children access social media through their phones. It is well-known that this age group is in a particularly sensitive phase of brain development, making them more susceptible to the content they encounter in a digital landscape that has few protective measures and many malicious actors aiming to influence young minds for financial gain. As a former digital marketing business owner and specialist, I am aware of the great lengths social platforms undertake to keep children engaged with their applications. They also allocate hundreds of millions of dollars to achieve this.

My proposition is not that children should merely place their smartphones in their pockets on silent mode while they vibrate constantly, or even just turn them off.

These devices remain too easily accessible, and students will find ways to bypass such rules because the allure of a peer seeking their attention or the number of likes on a social media post is an overwhelmingly powerful force.

It is necessary for us to implement the strategy used at concerts—securing them in a locked pouch at the start of the school day, which admittedly requires some effort. After such a policy is established, you would have the necessary time and resources to involve the SROs (school resource officers) in assisting with this process. I would also venture to guess they would be supportive, given that a significant number of school-related issues they address involve a smartphone.

Rephrasing the Concept: Moving Beyond the Term "Ban"

Perhaps for our discussion, we should avoid using the word "ban," as it can sound punitive. Alternative phrasing could be "Unplugged for Mental Health & Learning" or "Smartphone-Free School." These are merely suggestions, because the students, not the adults, should be the ones to name this initiative.

I anticipate some resistance to this idea.

Some express concern that restricting mobile phones will increase their desirability, similar to alcohol during the prohibition era.

This approach, however, does not entail complete prohibition—it is simply a temporary break. We can form a student advisory board to ensure they are invested in the experiment, preventing them from feeling that the rule is being imposed on them.

We could solicit their opinions on the potential adverse and beneficial results of spending time away from their devices. You might be surprised to learn that some children feel a sense of relief, though they may not openly express this to their peers.

Following this, we must conduct a survey after about a month to gather feedback.

  • How did the experience feel?
  • What were the negative aspects?
  • What were the positive aspects?

There could also be a panel discussion or a debate on the topic, allowing both sides to present their cases. We can then proceed based on the findings.

Another counterargument is that smartphones should be integrated into the curriculum to support educational objectives.

These are personal devices, and our educational programs have not yet evolved to a stage where this is a practical approach. At present, they primarily function as a source of distraction and do not contribute to a conducive learning atmosphere.

Why Do Parents Raise Objections to No-Cellphone Policies in Schools?

Despite a significant amount of evidence showing that mobile phones contribute to numerous youth mental health problems, some parents have resisted measures to limit phone access in schools.

The justification I most frequently hear from parents is the fear of school shootings or other tragic and violent incidents.

Parents desire the reassurance of an immediate message confirming their child's safety. I can certainly understand this sentiment. As a mother myself, I recognize that we live in a nation gripped by the fear of school shootings and violence.

First, it is important to remember that although the media heavily reports on school shootings, and they rightfully command national attention, such events are statistically infrequent.

A child's likelihood of being injured or killed by falling debris is greater than their chance of being killed in a school shooting. This is not to suggest we shouldn't be prepared, but how does a child possessing a phone at school prevent them from being harmed?

From Deputy David Gomez:

“Cellphones function like a drug, and children are biologically predisposed to addiction,” states Idaho’s Boise County Sheriff Deputy Dave Gomez. I now firmly recommend against children under 13 owning smartphones. I have witnessed too many 9 to 11-year-olds making decisions with lasting consequences on social media platforms they are not supposed to be using. Furthermore, a great number of parents have told me they were shocked by the amount of trouble their young children can get into online. If we want our children to learn effectively, we must remove those cell phones.”

Allowing Our Children to Have Phones in School Serves to Alleviate Parents' Anxieties

But at what expense does this come?

Are we so fearful of a hypothetical school shooting that we are willing to sacrifice our children’s mental, psychosocial, and academic development for the ease of contacting them for a dentist appointment pickup or in the aftermath of a disaster that has not happened? Please consider this. This practice serves only to mitigate the anxiety of parents.

Moreover, cell phones (specifically smartphones) contribute to the loneliness epidemic⁴ and diminish opportunities for children to develop coping mechanisms. How does this happen? The mobile phone acts as a crutch to evade uncomfortable face-to-face interactions, which in turn leads to fewer chances to cultivate resilience skills. Children today are not acquiring the coping skills that are learned through awkward and imperfect social exchanges.

If you were to cut open a cocoon to assist a caterpillar-turned-butterfly in avoiding the effort of emerging, you would deny it the capacity to fly. The very struggle to break free from the cocoon is what pushes fluid from the wings, thereby strengthening them and making flight possible.⁵

Key Arguments for Prohibiting Cell Phones (Smartphones) in K-12 Schools:

1. Phones Create Distractions from Learning

If our goal is for children to learn, it is imperative that we prohibit smartphones in schools.

  • Classroom disruptions: Cell phones disrupt lessons as students get notifications, text messages, and phone calls during class time. Even when not in active use, the vibration of the phone creates a temptation to check it, which redirects focus from the educational material.
  • Problems with multitasking: Students may attempt to multitask by sending texts, playing games, or using social media during class, which compromises their capacity to concentrate on the lesson, retain information, and engage in classroom activities.

"I have been employed at a high school for the last year, and I can confirm, as someone directly involved in this discussion, that there has been a significant enhancement in everything from academic performance to mental well-being. During the previous school year, we lacked a formal policy regarding mobile technology. In contrast, this fall, we introduced a no-cell-phone-during-instruction rule. This has been a transformative change."

Kristen, Staff Member at a High School in the USA

2. Mobile Phones Foster Addictive Behaviors

  • Screen Addiction⁶: If you attempt to take away a phone, some children will react with a full-blown tantrum. Providing your kids with a break from their phones and stating, “This is out of my control; it's the rule,” alleviates the pressure on you. Reduced screen time encourages them to use their minds and also lessens the exposure and associated mental health risks that come with everything accessible via a phone.
  • Other Addictions: An addiction to screens can pave the way for other addictions, such as substance abuse and gambling. Sports betting among university students is one of the most rapidly expanding addictions. My son Charles utilized his cell phone at school to arrange drug purchases and to provide illegal alcohol to other kids at parties for money to support his escalating substance abuse, which developed into a heroin addiction. Increased time spent on phones elevates the risk of engaging in problematic behavior.

3. Negative Impacts on Academic Performance

  • Phones decrease academic results: Research indicates that students who use their phones during class generally achieve lower academic scores. “Students who utilized their phones in class recorded fewer notes [7] and demonstrated poorer academic performance overall, in comparison to those who did not.⁷” Phones can disrupt both immediate comprehension and the long-term retention of knowledge.
  • Diminishes problem-solving abilities: Uninterrupted access to information through cell phones can discourage students from participating in more profound thought and problem-solving exercises. In essence, these devices impede the cultivation of critical thinking in teenagers, which is a vital skill for developing the resilience and coping strategies they will need throughout their lives.

4. Increases Instances of Cyberbullying and Harassment⁸

It is improbable that parents have been more successful at preventing cyberbullying at home than schools have been, yet parents frequently criticize schools for not taking greater action. While this may be valid in some instances, a significant amount of harmful activity occurs on the mobile phones that parents provide to their children, and these children need a respite from the constant harassment. They also require established boundaries. No educational institution can effectively monitor all the content that children access on these devices.

  • Elevated risk of bullying: Cell phones simplify the process for students to either perpetrate or fall victim to cyberbullying. They can send or receive malicious messages, disseminate rumors, or distribute inappropriate images.
  • Social exclusion: The use of social media and texting can foster the formation of cliques and exclusionary practices, where students might feel compelled to uphold a specific online image, thereby intensifying anxiety and peer pressure. There is also the threat of sextortion, a type of child sexual exploitation in which a young person is blackmailed, typically with the threat of publicly sharing their sexual images, by an individual who demands more sexual content, sexual acts, or money from the child.

5. Contributes to Mental Health Issues¹

  • Anxiety and depression: The overuse of cell phones, particularly social media, is associated with increased rates of anxiety, depression, and feelings of loneliness among students ¹, ⁴. The relentless comparison, the pressure to conform to which young people are prone, the fear of missing out (FOMO), and online criticism can all adversely affect mental health.
  • Sleep disruption: Students who take their phones to school might use them late into the night, which can disturb their sleep schedules and result in fatigue, diminished focus, and subpar academic results. They require a period of rest from these devices.

6. Fosters Cheating and Academic Dishonesty

  • Enables cheating: Cell phones provide effortless access to information during exams and quizzes, allowing students to find answers or distribute them to their classmates. This practice compromises academic integrity and the equity of evaluations.
  • Plagiarism: Students might use their phones to copy and paste text or concepts from the internet without giving proper credit, which further promotes academic dishonesty.

7. Can Create Safety and Security Problems

  • Reduced focus during emergencies: In an emergency situation, students might become panicked and try to reach their parents on their phones rather than adhering to the school's established emergency procedures. This could lead to confusion, obstruct evacuation processes, or escalate the severity of the situation.
  • Theft and damage: The presence of cell phones in schools can elevate the chances of theft, damage, or loss of personal items, which may result in disputes between students, parents, and the school.
  • Online Dangers: Roughly 40 percent of sex trafficking victims are recruited through online channels, establishing the internet as the most prevalent recruitment area.

8. Impedes Social and Emotional Growth

  • Diminished social interaction: When students are engrossed in their phones during breaks and unstructured time, it curtails face-to-face communication and chances to cultivate crucial social skills. This can negatively affect their capacity to communicate proficiently and form substantial relationships. How many children do you know who are capable of maintaining eye contact with an adult?
  • Reliance on technology: Permitting continuous access to phones can foster a dependency on technology as the main vehicle for communication and leisure, which may inhibit creativity and the development of independent thought.

9. Widens the Digital Divide Instead of Promoting Equal Access and Equity

  • Inequality in access: Not every student has access to the newest or most sophisticated smartphones. Those without may experience feelings of exclusion or disconnection, creating a perception of unfairness among their peers. It is well-established that connection and a sense of belonging are fundamental to positive mental health and well-being.
  • Economic burden: For families, the expectation that students need a phone to remain connected or to access educational resources can create a financial strain, especially for households with low incomes. (These devices are considerably more expensive than fashionable shoes or jeans.)

10. Creates a Distraction from School Policies and Objectives

  • Conflict with school policies: Schools frequently enforce stringent rules regarding acceptable conduct and the use of technology, and cell phones can pose a challenge to these rules, resulting in disagreements between students and teachers or school officials.
  • Eroding school culture: An excessive dependence on phones can weaken a school's culture of active participation, mutual respect, and focus, as students tend to become more withdrawn and less motivated to engage in school-related activities.

Establishing smartphone-free K-12 schools has been demonstrated to alleviate these problems, thereby cultivating a more focused, fair, and socially interactive educational setting.
