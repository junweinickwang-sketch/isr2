Presenting the Argument for a Ban on Phones in Educational Settings

Published on June 17, 2023 By Nathan Bowling

Ordinarily, I am hesitant to endorse “bans” as a remedy for any issue. I consider the War on Drugs to be one of the most profound injustices experienced in my life. It is my belief that banning gambling and sports wagering is nonsensical—what does it matter if a person decides to wager twenty dollars on the Timbers losing. The present wave of book prohibitions being advanced by conservative cultural advocates is repugnant and seems directly inspired by mid-20th-century European history. I perceived the recent congressional proceedings and discussions about prohibiting Tik Tok as a significant warning of America's descent into both governmental censorship and foolishness. There are many more examples I could provide.

Despite my general stance, I find myself in favor of a prohibition, or more accurately, a regulation on the time, place, and manner of teenage cell phone usage within schools. Since smartphones are a relatively new technology, a substantial body of long-term research on this subject is not yet available. However, the existing knowledge points to negative outcomes. The following is a summary from the National Library of Medicine:

Research from an observational study indicated that dedicating several hours weekly to electronic media was negatively associated with reported levels of happiness, life satisfaction, and self-esteem. Conversely, engagement in non-screen activities such as face-to-face social interactions, sports, exercise, reading print materials, doing homework, attending religious services, or holding a paid job showed a positive correlation with psychological wellness in adolescents. Additional observational research has connected more than two hours of daily use of social networking platforms and personal electronic devices to increased rates of suicidal thoughts and depressive symptoms in teenage girls. It was noted, however, that young people who maintained frequent in-person social engagement were comparatively shielded from the adverse effects of excessive online activity.

The finding that “in-person social interactions” positively affect well-being is critically important. To be clear, I am not advocating for a complete ban on internet access for children, in the vein of C. Delores Tucker, nor is this a diatribe against kids using the internet. Access to the online world, much like cured meats, can be seen as a wonderful benefit. My own life would be significantly less enriched without both the internet and salami. However, I would not attempt to live solely on a diet consisting of salami, prosciutto, chorizo, and bacon (and I would hope for an intervention if I tried). This analogy reflects my current position on this issue. An excessive number of students in countless schools are consuming a constant diet of screen time, from laptops in the classroom to social media outside of it. Educational institutions ought to be environments that foster positive social conduct and direct human connection. Prohibiting phone use on school grounds would serve to promote these very behaviors.

Many of you shared a similar sentiment. The following response from a reader, P.C., is indicative of the feedback I received:

For what it's worth, a key factor in our decision to select [redacted: school details] was their effective social media policy. A large board equipped with numerous pockets is positioned near the entrance, where students simply place their phones upon entering the classroom.

Voilà. It is incredibly straightforward for educators now, due to a very strict no-mobile-phone rule, and should you observe a student with their phone (whether in hand, on their desk, or elsewhere), it is promptly taken away and held in the principal's office for a minimum of one or two days (he has a reputation for being intentionally slow to contact parents and schedule a return) before it is given back.

And interestingly, severing that physical connection seems to result in the children being less attached to the devices even when they are not in the classroom.

Another reader, R.G., contributed this thought:

 This is crucial for the social and emotional health of these children. My own children [redacted identifying information] experience anxiety when needing to interact with others to handle tasks. We ALL share that anxiety. But since young people are constantly on their phones, typical social exchanges become difficult because they do not practice them frequently. The more one interacts with others, the easier it becomes.

While the idea may make some people uncomfortable, schools function as instruments and settings for socializing individuals into societal norms. The three choices that have most significantly enhanced my personal happiness were my marriage to Hope, the decision to pursue a teaching career abroad, and my departure from Twitter. I continue to have an online presence. It's clear I am not composing this on a typewriter in the style of Cormac McCarthy (whose passing we unfortunately mourned this week). Nevertheless, I believe that requiring students to put away their phones on school grounds would ultimately yield a similarly beneficial effect on them.
