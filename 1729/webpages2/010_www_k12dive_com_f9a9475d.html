Parental Resistance Grows Against School Cellphone Prohibitions

A recent survey from the National Parents Union shows that 78% of parents desire their children to have access to cellphones for potential emergencies.

Originally published on September 12, 2024
Kara Arundel Senior Correspondent

Dive Brief:

  • A growing number of school districts and states are implementing policies to restrict or prohibit cellphone usage during the school day, yet they are encountering increasing opposition from parents. These parents emphasize the importance of immediate communication during emergencies and express apprehension about disciplinary actions for non-compliance.

  • Recent survey data published on Monday by the National Parents Union revealed that 78% of participating parents believe their children should have access to a cellphone throughout the school day for emergency situations. Furthermore, 58% of respondents indicated that cellphone access is essential for parents to contact their children and confirm their location, while 48% stated that such contact is necessary for arranging transportation.

  • While numerous cellphone prohibitions are intended to enhance student mental health and academic concentration, a report from the Kaiser Family Foundation published this month notes that research supporting the efficacy of these bans is scarce. The report also highlights persistent difficulties related to enforcement, managing exceptions, and maintaining equity.

Dive Insight:

The polling data from the National Parents Union indicates that 62% of parents report their children are prohibited from using cellphones during classroom and instructional periods but are permitted to use them at designated times outside of class. The remaining 38% of parents state that their child's school enforces a total prohibition on cellphone use.

In addition, just 30% of all parents surveyed reported that their child's school sought parental feedback regarding its cellphone policy. Despite this, a majority of parents, at 71%, feel that their school's current cellphone policy is generally appropriate.

Keri Rodrigues, president of the National Parents Union, stated, "During school emergencies, the ability to communicate directly with your child to confirm their safety is of utmost importance, and frequently, schools are failing in their communication efforts." She added, "Policies that ban cellphones do not account for the tragic, real-world situations that regrettably occur far too frequently in educational institutions."

This poll from the National Parents Union was conducted using responses gathered from 1,508 parents who have children attending K-12 public schools.

The tragic mass shooting on September 4 at Apalachee High School in Georgia, which resulted in four fatalities and nine injuries, has intensified the ongoing discussion about the advantages and disadvantages of allowing students to possess cellphones on school grounds.

As of September 4, the KFF reports that seven states have enacted statewide policies to either prohibit or limit the use of cellphones in schools: Florida, Indiana, Louisiana, Minnesota, Ohio, South Carolina, and Virginia. These states are adopting varied strategies; some are banning usage, whereas others are limiting both the use and possession of devices.

For example, Indiana's policy forbids students from using any portable wireless devices, such as cellphones and laptops, during teaching hours without permission from educators or administrators. In contrast, Virginia's policy mandates that state officials gather public feedback on school cellphone use to help formulate definitions for “cellphone-free education,” as well as to release sample implementation strategies and proposed policy guidelines for school districts.

A number of local school systems have also instituted their own policies governing student cellphone usage. Notably, the Los Angeles Unified School District is currently formulating a cellphone-free policy, which is slated for implementation in January.

Previously, New York City Public Schools enforced a districtwide ban on cellphones, but this decision was overturned in 2015, granting individual schools the autonomy to establish their own rules. According to various news outlets, the district is now reconsidering the implementation of a new districtwide policy.

In areas where cellphone bans are in effect, some educators, students, and families have reported enhancements in academic achievement and social interaction. A survey conducted this spring with 2,889 members of the National Education Association showed that 90% endorse school policies that forbid cellphone and personal device use during instructional periods. Additionally, 83% favor a prohibition on these devices for the entire school day, allowing for exceptions related to medical or assistive technology requirements.

The official stance of the NEA regarding cellphones is that the responsibility for enforcing usage policies should not fall solely on individual teachers. The organization also advises that schools should refrain from measures that involve policing or actions that could lead to the criminalization of student conduct.

A 2023 study by Common Sense Media discovered that 97% of individuals aged 11 to 17 used their mobile phones at some point during school hours. The research, which analyzed smartphone data from 200 students, determined that the most common activities for students using their phones at school were social media (32%), YouTube (26%), and gaming (17%).

This past June, U.S. Surgeon General Dr. Vivek Murthy authored an opinion article for The New York Times in which he called for social media platforms to display warning labels similar to those found on alcohol and tobacco products. Echoing this sentiment, a group of 42 state attorneys general sent a letter to congressional leaders on Tuesday with the same request.

The letter stated, "Our youth are confronting a significant mental health crisis, largely driven by social media." It continued, "As acknowledged by Surgeon General Murthy, this widespread harm to a generation necessitates urgent measures.”
