Knitting, Cheerleading, and Fishing: A Look at How One School District Implements a Cellphone Ban

A Washington state district is observing reduced absenteeism and increased student engagement after combining its cellphone ban with an expansion of extracurricular activities.
by Neal Morton April 8, 2025May 9, 2025

SPOKANE, Wash. — Inside a science lab at Ferris High School on a Thursday, approximately 60 students spent their lunch period sorting through fishing lines and hooks, arranging reels and rods on desks for their weekly introductory angling session.

Later, at least one of these fishing enthusiasts joined the varsity cheerleading team for practice, a squad that, with a record-setting nine boys this year at Ferris High, secured a second-place finish in a statewide tournament the very next day. Demand for the sport has grown so much that middle schools now have waiting lists for students eager to join.

Simultaneously, at Longfellow Elementary School, pupils were busy in their knitting club, where they stretched yarn over improvised looms created from toilet paper rolls to stitch octopus arms or fashion a snake. “To be honest, it’s really fun, but it is difficult, and your fingers get sore,” commented fourth-grader Layden, holding a piece of thick red yarn.

These activities—such as angling at lunchtime, cheerleading waitlists, and elementary school knitting clubs—illustrate the practical application of the smartphone ban within Spokane Public Schools.

For years, this district, which is the third-largest in Washington state, considered implementing a smartphone ban for young people, joining numerous other school systems, states, and nations that were experimenting with similar policies due to rising concerns over the devices' effects on student mental health, social abilities, and academic performance. According to the Pew Research Center, approximately seven out of ten Americans endorse school cellphone prohibitions, at least during class time, while an additional one-third supports a ban that lasts for the entire school day. Such restrictions have even received praise from the new U.S. health secretary, Robert F. Kennedy Jr.

However, district leaders and community partners in Spokane aimed to do more than just take devices away from children. Their goal was also to involve young people in real-life experiences, a campaign the district has named “Engage IRL.” To achieve this, school officials developed a strategy to ensure every student participates in an after-school activity, club, or sport on a daily basis.

“Implementing the cellphone ban by itself is not enough,” stated Superintendent Adam Swinyard. “The objective here is to foster the development of healthy habits.”

When Spokane Public Schools students returned to class last fall, they were greeted with new regulations: pupils in elementary and middle school are required to store smartphones, watches, and other devices with internet access inside a backpack, pouch, or otherwise out of view. For high school students, the policy is more relaxed, permitting phone use during meal times and in the hallways between classes.

As part of its Engage IRL campaign, the district has broadened its extracurricular offerings across all 58 of its schools. Additionally, teachers are now permitted to organize as many as two field trips per month, and the city provides complimentary public transportation for these outings. A nearby nonprofit organization is contributing $3 million over a three-year period to fund “engagement navigators,” who monitor participation statistics at every school to identify new activities or simplify the process for students to get involved.

Officials from the district have reported what they believe are initial indicators of success for their dual-pronged strategy: By March, close to 18,000 students had taken part in an after-school club, sport, or activity, which represents a 19 percent increase over the entire previous school year. Furthermore, a district spokesperson noted that chronic absenteeism dropped by approximately 13 percent for students who were involved in an IRL activity.

Simultaneously, researchers have begun gathering data on the effects of smartphone restrictions in other locations, and recent findings suggest that classroom bans alone may not be sufficient to counteract the perceived negative consequences of the technology.

John Ketcham, who is a legal policy fellow at the Manhattan Institute, co-authored the conservative think tank's sample legislation for states that are contemplating restrictions on smartphone usage in schools. He emphasized that any such ban must be merely one component of a broader initiative to help disengaged young people reconnect with their surrounding community.

“After we break the cycle of habitual smartphone use among children, it will unveil new opportunities for them, including different methods of socializing and forming friendships,” Ketcham stated. “Investigating these possibilities after school can undoubtedly aid in providing children with wholesome alternatives.”

In 2015, prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, the typical teenager dedicated around 6.5 hours daily to screen-based entertainment like gaming and social media, as reported by the nonprofit Common Sense Media. By the year 2021, this daily usage had increased by two hours for teenagers, and this 8.5-hour total excludes any time spent using screens for schoolwork or homework.

A report from Common Sense Media last year also revealed that children are becoming engaged with technology at a young age: two out of every five children receive their first tablet by the age of two, and almost one-fourth own a personal cellphone before they enter the fourth grade.

At Spokane's Longfellow Elementary School, a survey conducted last year showed that approximately half of the students in third through fifth grade owned their own phone, as reported by Principal Adam Oakley.

“When we ask parents about the reason, it is always for safety, 100 percent of the time,” Oakley explained.

Parents report feeling more secure when they have a way to contact their children in an emergency situation. They can also use phones to send medication reminders, coordinate carpools, or inquire about after-school plans—all of which Oakley views as classroom disruptions. Teachers also find these interruptions unwelcome. A survey by Pew last year found that one-third of all teachers, and nearly 75 percent of high school teachers, identified smartphones as a "major problem" in their classrooms.

To date, a minimum of nine states have prohibited student devices during the school day, according to reports from the Associated Press. The Republican-led state of Florida enacted the first law of its kind in the nation, establishing a statewide ban, while California's Democrat-controlled legislature will mandate that all school districts create their own policies by the upcoming summer.

This movement has become global in scope. Brazil, Italy, the Netherlands, and various Canadian provinces have now joined an expanding roster of countries that have enacted extensive restrictions on smartphone use in schools. International research on the outcomes of some of the earlier bans has begun to emerge, revealing that in Norwegian schools, prohibiting smartphones led to a decrease in bullying and an improvement in academic grades for girls, especially for those from low-income families. A study in Denmark discovered that students engaged in more physical activity and expended more energy during recess when they did not have their phones.

However, a comprehensive study conducted in England found no correlation between a student's mental health, quality of sleep, or problematic social media use and their school's cellphone policy. The study's main author, Victoria Goodyear from the University of Birmingham, mentioned to the BBC that these results indicate that bans might not be effective when implemented in isolation.

“We must do more than simply prohibit phones in schools,” she remarked.

In Spokane, a district with approximately 29,000 students, overall attendance has seen a continuous decline each year following the start of the pandemic. This pattern concerned Ben Small, a former superintendent who currently directs LaunchNW, the educational division of a local philanthropic organization. He was even more troubled by youth mental health statistics: in 2010, only 54 children in Spokane County either attempted or died by suicide; by 2022, the combined number of suicides and attempts among children had climbed to 587.

“We are obligated to try a different approach,” stated Small, who presented the district with the concept of employing engagement navigators in 2022 to link students with after-school programs. “A sense of belonging is essential, and when that is formed solely in a virtual environment, it lacks authenticity. It is imperative that we refocus on nurturing face-to-face relationships.”

In contrast to the Manhattan Institute's proposed legislation for smartphone bans, Spokane’s policy does not link violations to disciplinary action. Each school is largely responsible for managing infractions on its own. Matthew Henshaw, the principal at Flett Middle School, stated that he allows teachers to handle the enforcement of the new rules, but he frequently has conversations with students who repeatedly violate them, and sometimes with their parents as well.

This academic year, Flett introduced new after-school programs for running, pre-engineering, beading, cooking, yoga, and Salish—a language spoken by Native American communities in eastern Washington. The main challenge has been securing enough adults to supervise these clubs.

Spokane Public Schools provides teachers with a payment of $28 per hour to supervise after-school programs. Support staff members are also eligible for extra compensation, yet the schools are struggling to find a sufficient number of community volunteers to satisfy the existing demand. On a national level, volunteerism has decreased since the pandemic, a trend that is not limited to the education sector.

Andrew Gardner serves as one of the five engagement navigators employed by LaunchNW. Each week, he travels between 11 different schools, including Ferris, where he analyzes participation data to pinpoint students who are not yet involved. A student's answer on a survey regarding popular activities can provide Gardner with a way to start a conversation.

“You indicated an interest in this at the beginning of the year. Are you still interested?” Gardner might say to an eighth-grader who is enthusiastic about a specific sport. “Let’s get you over to the high school. Let’s get you to a game, and let’s get you participating now.”

A frequent obstacle he has observed is older students who have responsibilities to babysit their younger siblings. To address this, Ferris now provides after-school programs tailored for these younger children, enabling the older students to take part in their own activities.

The district and LaunchNW have established agreements to exchange attendance data and mental health survey information to evaluate the effectiveness of the Engage IRL initiative. Principal Oakley, from Longfellow Elementary, provided an additional indicator of progress: last year, he confiscated two to three devices weekly during recess. This year, he did not have to confiscate any until the end of January.

Initially, during recess, students on the blacktop seemed unsure of what to do without their devices.

“Students had difficulty, I believe, in relearning how to play,” Oakley observed. “They do still remember how to play tag.”

The school has expanded its offering of structured games, such as flag football, during recess periods. Oakley also conducted a survey among teachers to gather their ideas and personal hobbies, subsequently enlisting them to launch new clubs. (He also expressed a desire for additional volunteers.)

About an hour after the final bell at Longfellow Elementary, a ball of yarn flew past the head of a father who was attending the knitting club with his daughter. Layden, the fourth-grader, was tossing the yarn across the classroom while she awaited assistance with a stitch. She also participates in basketball, football, and soccer at the school.

“It just looked like it would be fun, and I was interested in learning how to knit,” she explained. “I come here after school to relax. It is quite calming.”

Jetaime Thomas also maintains a packed extracurricular schedule.

As a senior at Ferris High, she holds the position of yearbook editor, serves as the varsity basketball manager, is the president of the Black Student Union, and is involved in student government as well as a comedy improv team. Thomas mentioned that she wished she had sufficient time to participate in the angling club and a creative writing group.

“I am involved in many activities, perhaps too many,” she commented. “It helps me stay engaged.”

With college applications weighing on her thoughts, Thomas noted that the district's initiative for more extracurricular activities came at a perfect time for building her resume. Her resume now highlights her contribution to organizing a Martin Luther King Jr. Day convention, or “MLK Con,” at Ferris High this year.

However, Thomas expressed concern that a federal push against DEI in education might threaten the existence of affinity groups like the one she leads.

“I feel anxious about what the next few years will bring,” she admitted. “At a high school that is predominantly white, finding a sense of community is what gets you through.”

She also acknowledged some benefits in the strategy of combining more clubs and sports with the smartphone restriction. Initially, Thomas herself was annoyed by the ban, and other students protested the mere idea of the school confiscating their property. This sentiment shifted over the course of a few months.

“I have been able to give 100 percent of my focus to each of my classes. It seems like people are more invested in class and more engaged,” she observed. She added that all the activities kept her away from social media, and her participation in them also fueled her ambition. “They also motivated me to continue striving.”
