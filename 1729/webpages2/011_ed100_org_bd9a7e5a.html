How Your School Can Prepare for the Upcoming Smartphone Ban

Authored by Carol Kocivar | Published on April 18, 2025 | 0 Comments

A New California Law Mandates Restrictions on Smartphone Use in Schools

It is no longer a matter of if your school should restrict smartphone usage, as this is now a legal requirement. The central question has shifted to how this policy will be implemented.

Under the California Phone-Free School Act, all school districts, charter schools, and county education offices are obligated to establish a policy that either limits or completely prohibits smartphone use. This mandate is not discretionary. Your school must have a comprehensible and acceptable plan in place by the deadline of July 1, 2026.

Understanding the Core Problem with Smartphones in Schools

A substantial body of evidence indicates that unrestricted smartphone access is detrimental to our well-being, particularly for children and teenagers. The California legislature enacted the Phone-Free School law swiftly and with overwhelming support, citing that the widespread presence of phones in schools diminishes student academic performance, facilitates cyberbullying, and is a factor in rising rates of anxiety, depression, and suicide among teens.

These devices are frequently cited as a principal cause for various negative consequences. For instance, higher education institutions have noted that since smartphones became commonplace, a significant number of incoming students lack the preparation needed for collegiate-level reading.

A Key Concern is Excessive Screen Time, Averaging 8 Hours Daily.

The amount of time students spend on screen media is considered excessive. This usage has increased substantially following the pandemic.

Research from Common Sense Media highlights a swift and concerning surge in the daily screen time of teenagers. Over 40% of adolescents are now engaged with screens for more than eight hours each day. This indicates that many young people dedicate more hours to digital entertainment than to sleeping, and these usage rates continue to climb.

An Official Warning from the Surgeon General

Such a high degree of fixation and distraction poses a health risk to everyone, yet it is particularly harmful for children. A formal report issued in May 2023 by the U.S. Surgeon General specifically addressed the potential for harm that social media poses to the mental health and overall well-being of young people and adolescents. This official advisory carries significant weight.

Known for his direct approach, Surgeon General Dr. Vivek H. Murthy has advocated for the placement of warning labels on social media applications. He stated, “The mental health crisis among young people is an emergency — and social media has emerged as an important contributor.” Furthermore, he recommended that “schools should ensure that classroom learning and social time are phone-free experiences.”

Increased duration of social media use correlates with a greater risk of self-harm, depression, and diminished self-esteem. For those interested in further exploration of this topic, the National Library of Medicine provides a wealth of research that is available for review.

Quantifying the Negative Impact of Smartphones in Educational Settings

The use of smartphones within schools is detrimental to students. A comprehensive report titled, The Status and Evidence for School Cell Phone Bans, enumerates several of the specific harms:

  • Classroom Distraction: According to a 2018 Pew Research Poll, 75% of high school educators identify cell phones as a significant source of distraction in their classrooms.
  • Reduced Attention: Research shows that students who do not have their phones present during lessons are able to record more information.
  • Lower Academic Performance: The PISA 2022 results indicate that students distracted by peers using digital devices achieved scores 15 points lower than students who were not exposed to such distractions.
  • Academic Dishonesty: At least 30% of students admit to using their cell phones for cheating in the classroom, a figure that is likely underreported.
  • Increased Loneliness: The availability of cell phones in schools is associated with higher instances of student loneliness; adolescents who feel lonely have a greater probability of leaving school at age 16.

State Governments Are Moving to Prohibit Cell Phones in Schools

Given the evident negative effects, countries and U.S. states are rapidly implementing policy measures, particularly within educational institutions. The health policy nonprofit KFF.org, located in San Francisco, is spearheading the collection of state-level data on the evolving smartphone policies in schools and their effects. The provided map illustrates KFF's data as of March 5, 2025 (you can click the map to view the most current information from KFF).

Determining the Next Steps for Your School

California legislation mandates the participation of parents and community members in creating school policies regarding smartphones. The first question is, what is the best way to begin this process?

Fortunately, a Playbook was released by the U.S. Department of Education in 2024 to assist communities in formulating these policies. The primary initial decision is to determine if the restrictions will apply on a schedule-based model or for the entire school day:

Schedule-Based Policies: These policies prohibit student device usage during specific periods of the school day, such as instructional time, but allow for restricted use during other times like breaks or the lunch period.

Full-Day Policies: These policies forbid the use of student devices throughout the entire school day, which encompasses all instructional time, lunch, free periods, and transitions between classes.

The following table offers guidance on additional significant questions that your school's administration and community must address:

Key Considerations for Developing Smartphone Policies

Safety

Does the school have an established emergency operations plan specifying how communication will be delivered to parents and caregivers during an emergency?

Has this emergency plan undergone testing to confirm its effectiveness in a crisis situation?

Are the expectations for using personal devices during a school emergency clearly defined?

Note: It is recommended to utilize the Department’s K-12 School and District Emergency Management Planning Tools.

Exceptions

State law in California permits student phone use during emergencies, with proper authorization, for reasons related to the student's health or well-being, or when a smartphone is a necessary component of a student's individualized education program (IEP).

Please refer to the section Designing Policies with Students with Diverse Needs, which can be found on page 21 of the Playbook.

Storage

Under what conditions might a device be confiscated from a student, and what is the designated storage location for such items?

What is the official policy for returning a confiscated device?

What methods will be used for students to store their devices so they are put away during class time (for example, using over-the-door organizers, baskets, or bookbags)?

Will a uniform storage method be adopted across all classrooms, or will individual educators have the discretion to choose their own storage solutions?

What procedures will be in place for students to store their devices for the entire day (such as in secure pouches, lockers, or bookbags)?

Note: It is important to evaluate the financial implications and determine who will bear the main responsibility for enforcement (such as teachers or school administrators) for each potential storage solution.

Cost

What is the financial expense required to provide every classroom with a solution for storing devices out of sight (like an over-the-door organizer)?

When using storage pouches, what is the annual cost associated with purchasing or leasing them?

Can this expense be sustained over the long term?

Who bears the financial responsibility for replacing a pouch if it is lost or damaged (for instance, the school or the student)?

Enforcement

Which individuals or groups are tasked with enforcing the policy?

Are the specific roles and duties of staff members clearly defined within their existing job descriptions?

Note: Various staff members, such as administrators, security personnel, office staff, or teachers, may have distinct roles. For instance, it's important to clarify who will check and seal pouches, collect phones, and administer consequences.

Training

What specific training is necessary for educators and staff to ensure the consistent and effective implementation of the personal device policy?

Responsible use

Have our expectations for the responsible use of both personal and school-provided devices by students been clearly defined? (A sample Responsible Use Policy is available for reference.)

What are the established protocols for getting permission from staff or fellow students prior to recording photos or videos?

Communication

What communication strategy will be used to inform students, families, educators, and staff, ensuring a universal understanding of the policy's expectations, requirements, and consequences?

Evaluation

What methods and frequencies will be used to collect data and feedback on our personal device policy to verify it is achieving its intended goals?

What systems for feedback can be created with students, families, educators, and staff to identify successes and areas needing adjustment due to new issues, technological advancements, or shifting educational goals?

The Role of Parents and Teachers in Policy Development

You should now contact your school district to inquire about their process for developing this policy. This process must include the participation of students, parents, and educators from your local community.

You should request that your school district guarantees adequate communication with teachers, students, and parents, and provides opportunities for the community to review the proposed policy.

For a school's smartphone policy to be truly successful, it is crucial that parents reinforce similar policies within the home. Guidance on this topic is available from the American Academy of Pediatrics, which offers advice for parents on collaborating with their children on Setting Limitations on Tech Time.
