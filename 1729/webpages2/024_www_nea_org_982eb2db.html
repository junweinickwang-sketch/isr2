Educators Evaluate the Advantages and Disadvantages as Schools Reconsider Cell Phone Bans

The trend of modifying cell phone policies is not exclusive to New York City. Across the nation, regulations are shifting, causing many educators to express apprehension about the potential impact on their learning environments.

By: Kinjo Kiema
Publication Date: February 23, 2015

Despite students consistently using mobile phones in their daily routines for nearly ten years, a significant number of public schools still resist the integration of these devices into the classroom setting. While educational institutions often struggle with adopting new technologies, the perception of cell phones as a primary source of nuisance and distraction has proven particularly difficult to overcome.

In recent times, nevertheless, there has been a noticeable increase in the acceptance of these devices. Starting in March, the New York City school district, which is the nation's largest with 1.1 million students, is set to overturn its long-held prohibition on cell phones within its schools.

This prohibition was first put into place by the Bloomberg administration and became effective in 2006; however, Mayor Bill De Blasio advocated for the change in policy, stating his belief that it is crucial for parents to have the ability to contact their children with ease.

Is it likely that additional school districts nationwide will adopt a similar approach? According to Liz Kolb, an assistant professor at the University of Michigan's School of Education and the author of Toys to Tools: Connecting Student Cell Phones to Education, this shift is already underway. Kolb reports that nearly 70 percent of educational institutions that enforced cell phone bans five years prior are now overturning those very policies.

Before the school day begins, students attending Norman Thomas High School in New York City are shown paying a one-dollar fee to store their electronic devices in a designated van.

Kolb elaborates, "Initially, the change resembled a very slow domino effect, but what we are witnessing now is more akin to a tidal wave." She clarifies, "This is happening partly because resisting this widespread trend is difficult, given the sheer number of students who own cell phones. Furthermore, schools are beginning to recognize these devices as valuable learning instruments, rather than mere entertainment gadgets, and they also see the potential for cost savings by not having to supply technology to every student."

Opponents argue, however, that permitting these devices in schools will inevitably promote their use for non-academic purposes, creating substantial distractions for both educators and learners, as well as offering a new avenue for academic dishonesty. A particular point of apprehension for both parents and educators is the possibility that removing the cell phone ban may lead to an increase in cyberbullying and sexting during the school day. In response, New York City school administrators are proactively implementing measures to address this issue, with the goal of reducing the overall incidence of sexting and cyberbullying.

Educators across the board continue to hold varied views regarding the presence of cell phones in educational settings. We recently posed this question on the NEA Today Facebook page, which prompted a diverse array of feedback.

The Concern of Student Cell Phone Dependency

Worries about cell phones in classrooms are also rooted in our understanding of the adolescent brain, which includes the difficulty of maintaining concentration while multitasking and potential long-term consequences for overall well-being.

In the view of Becky Dieffenbach, allowing students to bring their personal devices "simply creates a source of distraction for certain students, because regardless of how many times the rule is repeated that technology is only permissible when the teacher authorizes it, they opt to disregard the rule, which then requires disciplinary measures to be taken."

Connie Fawcett, a high school educator in Oklahoma, states, "Students consistently use them extensively for personal communication through social media when their focus should be on classroom activities."

Drawing from her own experiences, Fawcett observes that she "can identify very few positive results from having cell phones in the classroom, yet it is quickly becoming the new standard. Learning is poised to decline even further. Any educational benefits students might derive from using them for instruction will be negated by the distraction element, which seems to be far more engaging and satisfying for many."

Conversely, other teachers hold a different perspective and encourage their colleagues to embrace the unavoidable presence of cell phones in schools and to discover ways to integrate them effectively into the classroom.

Amber Schaefer, an elementary teacher in Minnesota, asserts, "We must cease resisting the technology and begin to adopt it." She continues, "The more we resist, the greater the divide we create between ourselves and our students. The time has come to integrate and work together, rather than to prohibit and penalize."

New York teacher Barbara McConnell adds, "It would be preferable if they were not a factor in the school environment, but since they are, we should leverage them to our benefit."

Looking beyond the classroom, a large number of educators contend that prohibiting any form of technology can exacerbate inequity. Specifically in New York City, the school's ban on cell phones was most rigorously enforced in schools equipped with metal detectors, which are also the schools with the greatest populations of low-income and minority students.

Jos√© Vilson, a middle school mathematics teacher in New York City and the author of This Is Not A Test: A New Narrative on Race, Class and Education, concurs that such prohibitions broaden the disparity between underprivileged students and their counterparts. He argues that forbidding cell phones merely restricts students' access to technology, a problem that particularly affects those in urban schools.

Implementing an end to the ban in New York will allow students to "bring their cell phones into the school building without being treated as criminals or having to pay an additional fee to store it outside at a cellphone truck," Vilson notes.

Vilson, who incorporates a variety of devices in his own teaching, does not think that phones introduce "a significantly greater distraction than what students already face. The outcome is contingent on how educators manage the entire situation."

Once the updated policy is enacted beginning in March, individual schools within New York City will have the authority to create their own distinct rules concerning cell phone use in the classroom. This will leave many teachers to decide how they will manage the presence of these devices in both hallways and classrooms.

Professionals in the field recommend a methodical and thoroughly considered strategy. Liz Kolb advises schools to "begin on a small scale."

She elaborates, "Do not feel compelled to have students use their devices constantly just because they are available. It is crucial for them to be deliberate and considerate, to proceed with small steps, and to establish a clear protocol with rules and frameworks for how students physically manage their devices within the classroom."

Kolb highlights several schools in Michigan that have implemented new, clear-cut rules and guidelines for teachers, which were formulated to cater to student needs while also taking into account the concerns of educators. For instance, signs are displayed on classroom doors to show whether students are permitted to use their devices. A green light symbol indicates that usage is allowed, while a red light symbol means it is not.

Kolb concludes, "This system ensures that students are aware of the expectations, and it concurrently grants teachers the autonomy to decide if they will incorporate cell phones into their lessons. It provides them with a degree of flexibility. While it is a very straightforward policy, it has also proven to be highly effective."
