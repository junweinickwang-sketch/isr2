Superintendent Reykdal Issues New Guidance for Schools on Limiting Student Cell Phone Use in Class

Media Inquiries

Katy Payne she/her
360-764-0201

August 28, 2024

OLYMPIA—August 28, 2024—In an effort to enhance student learning and support mental health, schools both statewide and nationwide are implementing updated policies that restrict the use of cell phones throughout the school day.

A 2022 study from the Pew Research Center indicates that smartphone ownership among teenagers has climbed to 95%, a notable increase from 73% in 2014–15. Across the nation, educators are requesting assistance with policies that curtail student cell phone access during class time, with the goal of minimizing distractions and fostering an ideal learning environment.

A 2024 survey conducted by the National Education Association revealed that 79% of teachers in schools permitting cell phone use report it as a major source of disruption. Moreover, the same survey showed that an overwhelming 90% of educators would back policies designed to limit student cell phone use while instruction is taking place.

The Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction (OSPI) has now released a policy brief for schools in Washington, recommending that school administrators develop and implement updated cell phone policies before the 2025–26 academic year begins. It is recommended that school administrators collaborate with their local communities during the upcoming year to formulate a plan that is best suited to their specific circumstances.

“Limiting cell phone usage in the classroom enhances concentration, boosts academic learning, benefits both mental and physical health, and alleviates the pressures associated with social media,” stated State Superintendent Chris Reykdal in the guidance document. 

OSPI's guidance advises against individual teachers creating their own cell phone rules, instead promoting the adoption of consistent, school-wide policies to establish clear and uniform expectations for all students, staff members, and families. 

School districts are advised to allow for exceptions to these comprehensive policies in situations where a teacher incorporates electronic devices into their instructional lesson plans. Additionally, it is mandated that school policies permit students with disabilities to utilize a device for learning support if it is specified in their Individualized Education Program (IEP) or by their IEP team.

“It is well-established that students achieve greater success when they are able to concentrate on their studies,” commented Larry Delaney, President of the Washington Education Association. “This new guidance offers educators the necessary backing to cultivate improved learning environments and marks a significant advancement toward establishing safer, healthier schools for our student population.”

Policies that regulate the use of cell phones in classrooms achieve maximum effectiveness when they are combined with additional strategies aimed at supporting student mental wellness and teaching students how to engage with digital media safely and responsibly. 

“Our primary goal is to ensure students are fully engaged and focused during class, and we acknowledge that cell phones can represent a substantial distraction,” remarked Joel Aune, Executive Director of the Washington Association of School Administrators. “It is our desire for students to cultivate beneficial habits and acquire digital citizenship skills that will be advantageous for their futures. I have full confidence that leaders at the school and district levels will thoughtfully put in place policies that foster learning and well-being for every student.”

The role of technology is fundamental to the student learning experience. OSPI is collaborating with educators throughout the state to embed media literacy and digital citizenship skills within the state's established learning standards for English language arts. In addition, students are being instructed on secure and ethical methods for utilizing artificial intelligence as a tool to enhance their education. The purpose of this guidance is to assist educators in minimizing distractions that occur during periods of instruction.

“Our call for school leaders to update their cell phone policies is an integral component of a wider strategy aimed at bolstering our students' mental health and equipping them for success in today's digital landscape,” Reykdal concluded.
