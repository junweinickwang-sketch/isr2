Unpacking the Reasons Driving the Ban on Cell Phones in K-12 Schools

An increasing count of states and educational institutions are instituting bans or limitations on mobile phones. Experts from Johns Hopkins, Carol Vidal and Annette Anderson, clarify the supporting research and share their views on these debated policies.

By Emily Gaines Buchler

/ Published Sept 23, 2024

During the current academic year, a significant number of adolescents and teenagers across the country are attending classes without their smartphones, devices that often feel like a natural extension of themselves.

Recently, states including Florida, Indiana, Ohio, South Carolina, and Louisiana have enacted legislation to prohibit or limit cell phone use in K-12 educational settings, and numerous school districts in many other states have also established policies to curtail their usage.

Image caption: Portraits of experts Annette Anderson and Carol Vidal

Within Maryland, over 20 school districts initiated programs at the beginning of the school year designed to ensure mobile phones remain powered off and stored away. As an example, Baltimore County Public Schools has launched a pilot initiative across 16 middle and high schools, mandating that students secure their devices in special "cell phone pouches" before each class begins. Likewise, high school students in Howard County are required to keep their phones in their backpacks, with usage permitted only during passing periods and lunch; conversely, elementary and middle schoolers are completely forbidden from using them, barring an administrator's approval for a specific event or as a reward for good conduct.

What are the primary motivations behind this wave of new policies?

A growing body of research indicates that smartphone usage could be contributing to the nation's youth mental health crisis, and it is also linked to school-related issues such as declining attention spans, reduced academic engagement, and hindered social-emotional growth.

For a discussion on the research and related policies, the Hub convened with Carol Vidal, a child and adolescent psychiatrist at the Johns Hopkins Children's Center and an assistant professor of psychiatry and behavioral sciences at the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, and Annette C. Anderson, deputy director of the Johns Hopkins Center for Safe and Healthy Schools and an assistant professor in the Johns Hopkins School of Education. This abridged transcript of their dialogue features Vidal and Anderson elaborating on their research findings and providing insights from their direct experiences with children and teenagers in school environments.

Dr. Vidal, your study published this May in the International Journal of Mental Health and Addiction examines the connections between social media engagement and mental well-being. Could you share what your research uncovered?

Vidal: A significant portion of the conversation about social media usage centers on its potential role as the cause of the ongoing youth mental health crisis, which is characterized by rising rates of depression and anxiety. In my assessment, I do not believe this is the correct conclusion. Our study's findings, which align with the majority of existing research, demonstrate that individuals experiencing higher levels of depression are also more inclined to use social media frequently. This correlation, however, does not necessarily imply a causal relationship between the two. It simply indicates that individuals with high or problematic social media usage often exhibit greater symptoms of depression. Yet, it remains uncertain whether one directly causes the other. For instance, our research found that social media use was not a predictor of increased depression over the study's duration.

"In the end, it is essential for everyone—including healthcare professionals, teachers, parents, relatives, and friends—to promote a healthy equilibrium between moderate screen time, social media engagement, outdoor activities, and physical exercise in our current digital era."
Carol Vidal
Johns Hopkins Children's Center

Nevertheless, it is evident that social media serves as a tool providing access to an extensive variety of content. Furthermore, it is purposefully engineered with features designed to be addictive. The objective of developers and designers is to keep users actively engaged with their platforms, as this is their primary method of generating revenue. Their aim is to present users with a continuous flow of material. When the content is more compelling to the user, they remain engaged for longer periods and are exposed to more advertisements.

An additional discovery from the research is that immoderate social media usage is associated with detrimental behaviors impacting both mental and physical well-being. Such behaviors encompass staying awake late into the night and reducing time spent outdoors, both of which are linked to internalizing disorders like depression and anxiety that manifest as emotional distress. The prevalence of these disorders has risen among young people over the past two decades, a period during which they have also spent more time indoors rather than engaging with the outside world.

How can one identify an addiction to social media?

Vidal: A substantial proportion of social media users exhibit problematic usage patterns, signifying that they interact with these platforms in unhealthy manners, constantly thinking about them and allowing them to dominate their lives. They also engage with it to an excessive degree and often feel a lack of control over their own usage. This behavior detracts from activities that are more beneficial for their personal development, like being outdoors, obtaining adequate physical exercise and rest, concentrating on academics and work, and enjoying leisure reading. The risk is particularly high for children and adolescents, as their brains are in a developmental stage and they possess diminished impulse control.

This presents a challenge for young individuals because social media is frequently their primary mode of socialization. Their interpersonal connections often incorporate and can even be centered on social media platforms. Consequently, moderating social media use becomes a difficult task for children, teenagers, parents, and educators alike.

Research indicates that addictions to social media and other screens share similarities with substance addictions, such as those to drugs or alcohol. The neurotransmitter dopamine is implicated in all forms of addiction. Consistent with other addictions, engagement on social media—through comments, likes, and similar interactions—can trigger a release of dopamine, functioning as a reward that temporarily stimulates the brain's pleasure centers. This is the mechanism through which individuals become addicted.

Dr. Anderson, could you describe the consequences when children and teenagers are given access to this potentially addictive media in a school setting through their cell phones?

Anderson: I would like to begin by stating that the primary reason most parents provided their children with cell phones was for immediate communication access. During this period of concern over school shootings, phones emerged as a critical safety tool and an essential device for managing the complex schedules and daily lives of contemporary families. The intention was never to provide children with access to video games or social media platforms such as Instagram and Snapchat. This outcome was an unforeseen side effect.

"According to school administrators I've spoken with, the effects of cell phones are visible on a daily basis. They report that our students exhibit higher levels of anxiety and depression and are more hesitant to take social risks to form new connections."
Annette C. Anderson
Johns Hopkins School of Education

However, the reality that parents and educators are observing is that smartphones have introduced a wide array of difficulties. There are instances of students recording and sharing inappropriate video content. Altercations in schools frequently gain viral attention due to students filming and subsequently publishing the footage on the internet. Furthermore, students experience distractions in the classroom as a result of their addiction to applications designed to constantly demand their focus.

The way our youth sustain their attention is also being altered by smartphones. They are frequently distracted, and it can appear as if nearly everyone has ADHD due to the continuous barrage of images, sounds, and various stimuli that may be hindering their ability to focus in an academic environment.

Moreover, children and teenagers are not the only ones affected. A large number of adults also sleep in close proximity to their smartphones. We are all experiencing the negative repercussions. Important family rituals like mealtimes, which provide an opportunity for children and caregivers to connect and discuss their day, are diminishing, primarily as a result of phone use.

What is the effect of student cell phone usage in classrooms on educators? What feedback are you receiving from school administrators and teachers, and what are your direct observations in schools?

Anderson: A great number of educators have informed me that they find it impossible to perform their professional duties effectively due to incessant disruptions. Students are distracted regardless of whether they are using earbuds or headphones. Our society is failing to uphold the principle that school time is exclusively for learning, as this environment has been compromised by a relentless stream of images, sounds, and activities that divert attention from the core educational mission.

Beyond the impact on academics, mobile phones are damaging social connections and impeding the developmental ability of young individuals to find pleasure in conversation and learn how to facilitate group participation. This phenomenon is clearly observable in the school cafeteria, where students are typically looking at their phones rather than engaging in the practice of social skills, which includes turn-taking, considering social dynamics, demonstrating empathy, and initiating conversational topics. These foundational elements of American society are experiences that our youth are currently being deprived of.

Administrators at schools report to me that they witness this impact on a daily basis. Our student population is experiencing increased anxiety and depression, and they are showing less willingness to engage in social risks to meet new people. Consequently, their social circles are becoming more limited than what the school environment is designed to foster. A school should be a place that promotes the highest level of engagement and social interaction. At times, especially for introverted individuals, using a phone can become a convenient way to avoid practicing the essential social skills required for future success in professional and public settings. Given these factors, it is understandable that school leaders feel they have exhausted their options and are determined to preserve these fundamental social norms.

What measures can families and educators take to assist children and adolescents in establishing boundaries for screen time, both within and outside of the school environment?

Vidal: Because addictions to smartphones and social media are not currently classified as formal disorders in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, making a diagnosis is complex. However, the critical factor to monitor is whether the behavior negatively impacts a person's daily functioning. Should children or teenagers begin neglecting their academic work or personal responsibilities, staying up late, or skipping meals, and if family interventions to moderate usage have been unsuccessful, these could be indicators that professional help from a school counselor or psychologist is needed. For instance, with screen addictions such as video gaming, some children might skip meals and play games throughout the night. I have personally observed this type of behavior in some of the patients I treat. Additional signs that require attention include a reduction in time spent outdoors and a degree of social withdrawal.

Ultimately, it is crucial for everyone involved—including healthcare professionals, educators, parents, family, and friends—to advocate for a balanced lifestyle that includes moderate use of screens and social media, combined with outdoor activities and exercise in our modern digital world. Implementing restrictions on cell phone use in schools represents a positive step, offering children and adolescents a chance to learn new ways of engaging with their surroundings. Typically, when an individual tries to cease using alcohol or other addictive substances, the initial action is to remove the substance from their home environment. I believe the essential strategy for managing screen addiction is moderation, which can be best accomplished by temporarily limiting smartphone access in specific settings; this prevents students from being constantly tempted to use their devices out of boredom or anxiety and enables them to engage more deeply with their studies, school personnel, and peers.

Anderson: A large number of children today experience high levels of stress and a sense of disconnection. This situation contributes to the increase in vaping and opioid use, highlighting the need for educators to have effective tools for discussing these and other important topics in a manner that resonates with and engages students. Achieving this is the objective of the Johns Hopkins Health Education and Training (HEAT) Corps, an initiative within the School of Education where I hold the position of curriculum director. Through the HEAT Corps program, we provide teachers with the necessary awareness and resources to comprehend the diverse challenges today's students encounter, including long COVID, gun violence, e-cigarettes, cyberbullying, and social media pressures.

I believe it is vital, and it is a core component of the HEAT Corps' mission, to empower young people to become advocates for their own responsible cell phone use. Our culture has transitioned from a time when television programming concluded at 11 p.m., signaling bedtime, to an era of continuous 24-hour content. We have radically changed our access to entertainment but have failed to introduce the corresponding education needed to manage it. Currently, there is a demand for everything to be available instantly, which extends beyond entertainment to include deliveries and educational content. Our expectation for immediate results has grown, while our ability to focus on a single subject for an extended period has significantly decreased.

A primary difficulty that arises when schools confiscate cell phones is the reduced ability of families to make immediate contact with their children. Observing social media discussions following the recent school shooting in Georgia reveals that parents are expressing considerable panic regarding school policies that ban cell phones. These parents are resistant to the idea of their children relinquishing their mobile phones.

A potential solution involves creating a device that serves exclusively as a telephone. An alternative approach is to implement software within schools that deactivates applications while preserving the ability for students to communicate with their guardians. The effectiveness of the current method—securing phones in pouches, backpacks, or lockers—is still uncertain, as is the likelihood that determined students will circumvent these regulations. For this reason, providing education and fostering awareness are crucial for helping children develop into self-advocates. When they become adults, they will have limitless digital content accessible to them instantly. It is imperative that we teach them healthy habits and how to set boundaries at this present time.
