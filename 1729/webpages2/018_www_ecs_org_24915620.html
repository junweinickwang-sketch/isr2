What Are the Current State-Level Strategies for Managing Cell Phone Use in Schools?

In an outdoor school corridor, two high school students are seen talking and using their cell phones near their lockers, with other students walking in the background.
Authored by: Erin Whinnery
Authored by: Erin Whinnery
Published on: Oct. 28, 2024
High SchoolK-12

The content of this article was compiled utilizing research conducted by Tom Keily, Zeke Perez, and Eric Syverson.

In recent years, leaders at both the school and state levels have taken action due to worries over the impact of cell phone use on both students and educators. Issues such as student data privacy, social media engagement, mental health effects, and distractions from instructional time have raised questions regarding how state policy can effectively support school responses. 

A 2022 study by the Pew Research Center reveals that an overwhelming majority of teenagers report access to a digital device, with 95% having a smartphone and 90% a computer, while 97% of teens indicate they use the internet daily. The study also found that 46% of teens reported being online 'almost constantly.' Furthermore, data from the National Center on Education Statistics shows that by 2022, nearly 77% of schools had implemented a ban on cellphone use.

A review of state policies, state education agency materials, and local education agency guidelines indicates that while school cell phone use has gained national focus, the matter is typically handled at the local district level. In cases where this issue is addressed at the state level, the policies generally: 

  1. Establish a broad definition for what is considered misuse of a cell phone.

  2. Mandate that local agencies create and adopt their own acceptable use policies.

  3. Forbid the use of cell phones during the administration of statewide tests. 

Only a small number of states specifically regulate the use of cell phones within classrooms. Our analysis of existing and proposed state legislation shows that states frequently manage student cell phone use through local student conduct policies or by requiring local education authorities to formulate and enact a policy. The following section provides examples of enacted state-level policies concerning the possession or use of cell phones in educational settings.

Alabama: The state board of education passed a resolution in February that strongly urges local school boards to implement policies restricting cell phone use on school premises.

California: A.B. 272, which was enacted in 2019, grants authority to the governing bodies of school districts, county education offices, and charter schools to create policies that either limit or completely prohibit students from using smartphones on school property. Exceptions are made for emergencies with employee permission or when usage is permitted through an individualized education plan.

Florida: H.B. 379, enacted in 2023, forbids students from using their cell phones during class instruction and mandates that teachers provide a designated storage area for these devices during instructional periods.  

Indiana: S.B. 185, enacted in 2024, obligates all public schools and charter schools to formally adopt, execute, and make public a policy that governs student use of any wireless communication device, which includes cell phones, tablets, laptops, and gaming systems. 

Kentucky: According to Rev. Stat. Ann. § 158.165, each school district's board of education is required to formulate a policy addressing the possession and use of cell phones by students on school property or at school-sponsored functions. This policy must then be incorporated into the district’s official standards for student conduct. 

Ohio: The enacted H.B. 250 (2024) mandates that school districts establish a cell phone policy designed to curtail cell phone usage throughout the school day.  

South Carolina: The state's Code Ann. Regs. 43-279 (IV)(A)(2)(j)
