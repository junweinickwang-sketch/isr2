The Controversy Over Cell Phones in Schools

Examining the Arguments: Should Schools Ban Cell Phones?

This information was last updated on: Aug 7, 2025 Article History

By the year 2024, fifty years after the invention of the first mobile phone, cell phone ownership in the United States reached 98 percent of the population, with smartphones accounting for 91 percent of these devices. This high rate of ownership is distributed evenly across various demographics, including gender, race, age, and political party. [1][2][3][4]

On a global scale, 2024 saw 4.88 billion smartphone users, representing approximately 60 percent of the world's population, with about 7 billion total smartphones in circulation. The country with the most smartphone users was China, with nearly 975 million, followed by India at 659 million and the United States at 276 million. It is projected that the number of smartphone users will grow by over 30 percent by the year 2029. [5]

The proportion of American children who use cell phones is increasing just as quickly as the age of initial exposure to digital media is decreasing. Recent research indicates that by age two, 40 percent of children possess their own tablet; this figure rises to 58 percent by age four. By age eight, 25 percent have a cell phone, which increases to 42 percent having a smartphone by age ten, and 91 percent owning a smartphone by age 14. This trend of early smartphone adoption is reflected internationally. In the United Kingdom, 97 percent of children have smartphones by age 12, while in Spain, 75 percent of children in the same age group own them. [6][7][8][90]

A significant majority of American students aged 11 to 17, specifically 97 percent, acknowledged using their mobile phones during school hours for an average of 43 minutes each day. Regarding the content accessed on their phones at school, 32 percent viewed social media (with TikTok being the most frequent), 26 percent watched YouTube videos, and 17 percent engaged in playing games. [9]

A federal survey revealed that during the 200910 academic year, 91 percent of schools in the U.S. had banned non-academic cell phone usage. As educational applications became more common, however, schools eased their technology restrictions, leading to only 66 percent of schools limiting phone use by the 201516 school year. This easing of rules was short-lived, as by the 201920 school year, the number of school cell phone bans increased again, with 76.9 percent of schools adopting such policies. The latest figures from the 202021 school year indicate a minor decrease to 76.1 percent. [10][11]

Indeed, amid rising cell phone use by students and growing apprehension about the impact of social media on adolescents, along with the release of social psychologist Jonathan Haidt's influential book The Anxious Generation (2024), state governments have started to implement or strongly recommend restrictions on cell phone possession and usage during school hours. Haidt has been a long-time advocate for phone-free schools and reduced technology use among children and teenagers, describing the smartphone as "a tool of mass distraction." His book has had a particularly strong impact in the K12 education sector, leading to nationwide town hall meetings on the topic, with many educators crediting The Anxious Generation for shifting their perspective. [12][13][14][15]

As of May 30, 2025, a minimum of 23 states in the U.S. had established some form of guidance restricting cell phone use. These policies typically allow for exceptions for students with individualized education programs (IEPs) and 504 plans, which provide accommodations for those with disabilities and other learning requirements, as well as for students whose medical conditions, such as diabetes, are managed with the help of phones. [12][13][14][15]

Prohibiting cell phone usage in schools is a global trend. A 2023 report from UNESCO indicates that nearly a quarter of all countries have implemented bans on cell phones in educational settings, with such prohibitions being more prevalent in nations across Central and South Asia. [16]

Regardless of the country, cell phone prohibitions typically fall into one of several fundamental categories:

  • Complete Prohibitions: Students are forbidden from possessing cell phones, and likely other Internet-enabled devices, on school property. Any prohibited devices are confiscated with varying consequences, such as being returned at the end of the day, requiring a parent to retrieve them, or holding the device until the end of the school year.
  • Bell-to-Bell Prohibitions: Students are restricted from accessing their cell phones from the first bell signaling the start of the school day until the final bell marking its conclusion. While phone use is often permitted during after-school programs, it is generally not allowed during lunch or other instructional breaks. Phones must either be stored in a designated area like lockers or backpacks, or secured in locking pouches that are kept by the student or in a central location.
  • Partial Prohibitions: Students are permitted to use their phones during non-instructional periods, such as in between classes or at lunchtime. In these cases, students are usually required to silence their phones or switch them to airplane mode and may need to keep them in bags or lockers.

Policies regarding cell phones also commonly extend to other Internet, Bluetooth, or cellular-connected items, such as earbuds, tablets, smartwatches, and basic non-smartphones (often called "dumbphones"). The latter two have recently been promoted to parents as alternatives for children deemed too young for a smartphone but who require a communication device. Certain policies also incorporate exceptions that allow teachers to authorize the use of phones and other devices for instructional purposes during class. [17][18]

It is important to note that the discussion surrounding cell phone bans in schools has significant overlap with broader debates about social media, which encompass issues from mental health to national security, including the use of widely used applications like TikTok.

Therefore, should cell phones be prohibited in schools? You can explore the different sides of this debate below.

Arguments For a Ban

 (Go to Arguments Against a Ban)

Pro Argument 1: Banning cell phones enhances student protection from physical, mental, and digital harm.

The presence of cell phones in educational environments can cause minor conflicts to escalate significantly. Cyberbullying has become an instantaneous and pervasive issue, capable of spreading in seconds through social media to the entire student body and beyond. [10][19]

Cell phones are increasingly being utilized to escalate disagreements to the brink of physical violence. Shari Obrenski, who is the president of the Cleveland Teachers Union, noted that high school students employ their cell phones to arrange, film, and share fights on social media, stating, "They were using the phones to create chaos and perpetuate violence in our schools." [10][19]

After examining over 400 videos of school fights, The New York Times "identified a trend of middle and high school students using phones and social media to orchestrate, incite, record, and circulate footage of violent altercations among their peers." In some cases, students involved later succumbed to their injuries. [20]

Journalist Natasha Singer reported, "School officials indicated that the rapidly evolving technology habits of young people have complicated efforts to prevent and manage student aggression. Many students now favor more private communication methodssuch as Snapchat, iMessage, and Apples AirDrop file-sharing serviceto organize and distribute fight videos, rather than using platforms like Instagram or TikTok." [20]

This indicates that policies banning social media or blocking student devices from accessing the schools Wi-Fi network are not sufficient. In contrast, a ban on cell phones entirely eliminates this avenue for escalating conflicts.

Furthermore, banning cell phones offers individual students a shield from physical and emotional distress. One study revealed that "when iPhone users were prevented from answering their ringing device while completing a word search puzzle, their heart rate and blood pressure rose, and their self-reported feelings of anxiety and unpleasantness intensified." The sound of an unanswered phone is particularly effective at triggering FOMO (fear of missing out) in young people. There is no reason for children and teenagers to face such stress and anxiety. Removing phones completely provides students with both literal and figurative space to breathe, away from their devices. [21]

Lastly, cell phone prohibitions serve to protect the privacy and data of students. An increased number of users on a Wi-Fi network renders it more susceptible to cyberattacks due to issues like network congestion, weak security measures (for example, IheartDavid is not a strong password), and a greater probability of someone clicking on a phishing link. Such attacks can compromise not only the student data held by the schoolincluding addresses, birth dates, and social security numbersbut also the data on the students' own devices, thereby exposing a broad range of personal information from private conversations to financial and medical records. [22][23][24]

Pro Argument 2: Banning cell phones serves to reduce distractions for both students and educators.

The "brain drain" hypothesis suggests that "the simple presence" of a mobile phone occupies cognitive resources, which results in a diminished capacity and analytical skill for other activities. Even when an individual is successfully concentrating on a given task, the mental energy required to resist the urge to check the phone diminishes both the available working memory capacity and the functional fluid intelligence. These adverse cognitive effects are most pronounced for individuals with a high degree of smartphone dependency. [25]

To put it plainly, humans are not capable of multitasking. An individual's functional IQ decreases by 10 points when attempting to do so, an effect comparable to that of a full night of sleep deprivation and more than twice the impact of smoking marijuana. Managing multitasking and resisting temptations is particularly challenging for young people, as their brains and emotional maturity are still developing. Implementing restrictions on when and where students can use cell phones lessens the temptation to multitask and cuts out the constant notification alerts from apps and messages, which have a distracting Pavlovian influence on people of all ages. [18][26][27][29][30]

Researchers have discovered that "clearly defined and safeguarded periods of separation" from cell phones can lessen their "brain drain" effect. Therefore, requiring students to place their cell phones in lockers, secured phone bags, or another specified location (rather than their own school bags or pockets) enables them to concentrate on their studies and face-to-face school interactions. [25]

Psychiatrist Candida Fink further explained, "For students with learning differences and conditions such as ADHDor for any student struggling with their studiesthe urge to check their phone instead of engaging in class is immense. Although these students might disengage in other ways, having access to a constant stream of algorithm-driven content pulls them into a digital rabbit hole that is very difficult to escape. While phones may be useful for certain learning activities for students with disabilities, unrestricted access is detrimental to learning and participation." [31]

A vast majority of high school instructors (72 percent) and American adults in favor of cell phone bans (91 percent) consider cell phones to be a source of distraction in schools. Some students also share this view, such as Emmies, a high school student from Atlanta, who commented, "If previous generations could go 8 hours without checking an Instagram story, then we can too." [32][33][34]

Pro Argument 3: Implementing cell phone bans helps to support and achieve educational objectives.

Although not everyone is in favor of a complete prohibition on cell phones, 68 percent of adults in the United States do support a ban on their use during instructional periods. [32]

One study revealed that "students who refrained from using their mobile phones recorded 62% more information in their notes, took more thorough notes, were able to recall more specific details from the lecture, and achieved a full letter grade and a half higher on a multiple-choice test compared to those students who were actively using their mobile phones." [35]

There is a wealth of studies providing supporting evidence that students of all ages and from various countries perform better on academic assignments, such as quizzes, when they do not have their cell phones (or other connected devices like smartwatches) in their possession. [36][37][38][39][40]

Additionally, half of American adults who are in favor of cell phone bans believe that a key reason for restricting cell phone use during classes is that students are less inclined to cheat when they do not have access to a cell phone. [32]

With nearly all information available at their fingertips on an Internet-enabled phone, students have the ability not only to find answers but also to employ AI chatbots to produce text, thereby bypassing in-class writing tasks.

Researchers also discovered that by removing cell phones from the classroom for students who are underachieving and those with special needs, an additional hour of instructional time per week (which amounts to about five extra days per school year) was gained, time that would have otherwise been spent managing cell phone use and digital interruptions. [41]

Quotes Supporting a Ban

Author AnneMoss Rogers has expressed:

We understand that this age group is in a more delicate phase of brain development and is consequently more vulnerable to the content they encounter in a digital environment with minimal safeguards and numerous malicious entities aiming to influence young minds for financial gain. As a previous owner and expert in a digital marketing business, I am aware of the great lengths social media platforms go to in order to keep children engaged with their apps. And they invest hundreds of millions to achieve this.

I am not suggesting that children should simply place their smartphones in their pockets on silent mode while they constantly vibrate, or even just turn them off.

They remain overly accessible, and students will find ways to bypass these regulations because the allure of a peer seeking their attention or the number of likes on their social media post is overwhelmingly powerful.

It is necessary for us to implement the method used at concertsplacing them in a locked pouch at the start of the school day, which, admittedly, requires some effort. Once they are prohibited, you would have the time and resources to involve the SROs (school resource officers) in this process. And I am confident they would be agreeable, considering that many of the school issues they handle are related to smartphones. [50]

Dr. Marty Makary, a physician and professor at the Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, remarked:

The children of America are suffering. Falling behind in their education after the pandemic-related school closures, many children are now facing another significant obstacle to their learningsmartphone addiction. Two-thirds of American students report being distracted by their digital devices while in class.

There is also a second-hand smoke phenomenon: according to a 2022 Program for International Student Assessment study, more than half of students are distracted by the devices of their peers. This is a multifaceted issue. However, one solution is remarkably straightforwardprohibit phones in America's classrooms.

As a society, we do not permit alcohol or drugs in schools. Why then should we allow the use of highly addictive phones in the same environment? [51]

The Los Angeles Times Editorial Board commented:

It should be evident by now that possessing a pocket-sized entertainment device that constantly buzzes with notifications and temptations is not conducive to children's ability to concentrate and learn. (It is not beneficial for adults either.)...

It is apparent that the presence of cellphones on campus is more detrimental than beneficial. Children require an intervention, and schools are correct to regulate this technology now, before another generation is affected.

Indeed, it will be challenging to alter the habits of both students, who are reluctant to be without their phones, and their parents, who are used to being able to contact their children at any moment of the day. It is also true that some students will attempt to circumvent the rules. The initial weeks and months of a cellphone ban will prove difficult for teachers, administrators, students, and parents. This will represent a significant cultural shift, but it is one that is worthwhile.

And it is quite plausible that by the school year's end, students and educators will reflect and wonder, "Why did we not do this sooner?" [52]

Author Jonathan Haidt made the following statement:

No educational institution ever expresses regret after becoming phone-free.

The advantages are substantial and manifest quickly.

Inform the head of your childs school that you desire for children to pay attention to one another and to their teachers, not to their phones. [53]

Arguments Against a Ban

 (Go to Arguments For a Ban)

Con Argument 1: Prohibiting cell phones can obstruct essential communication channels during emergency situations.

In any type of emergencyranging from a school shooting to a panic attackit is crucial that students have the ability to quickly and directly contact emergency services and their parents or other guardians if necessary.

While a majority of American adults who are against cell phone bans (60 percent) share this view, even higher proportions of adults who are likely to have a child in school are in agreement: among those who oppose the ban, 68 percent of individuals aged 18 to 29 and 63 percent of those aged 30 to 49 feel that parents should have the ability to reach their child by cell phone during school hours. [32]

Carline Dumoulin, a mother of two high school students in Florida, stated that cellphones are "the means for us to communicate if there are any kinds of issues happening at school, and I don't need to elaboratewe are aware of what has occurred in the past." [42]

Dumoulin is making a reference to the 2018 shooting at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School in Parkland, where 14 students lost their lives. During the incident, students had their cell phones with them, which not only enabled them to communicate with their families but also provided prosecutors with a significant amount of evidence for the shooter's trial. [42]

Although major emergencies might be infrequent, "this is a generation that has been raised with a notable rise in school shootings and practices like ALICE [alert, lockdown, inform, counter, and evacuate] drills," notes Meryl Alper, an associate professor of communication studies at Northeastern University. Since "these threats are very prominent in the minds" of younger generations, simply having a cell phone accessible may help to alleviate some anxiety, which could in turn enable students to concentrate better on their schoolwork. [12]

The reality is that cell phones have become a fundamental component of contemporary life. Very few adults venture out without a cell phone. Why should we expect children and teenagers to behave any differently? As Keri Rodrigues, the president of the National Parents Union, pointed out, "All families depend on cell phones to remain connected and communicate now more than ever. Whether it is for making contact in an emergency, coordinating after-school arrangements, or even if a child is feeling anxious about an issue at school and requires encouragement or adviceit is evident that parents wish to maintain clear and direct lines of communication with their own children. An outright ban on cell phones in school or treating them as prohibited items, rather than employing effective classroom management, is completely unreasonable and detached from the reality we live in." [43]

Instead of enforcing a ban that is not practical, individual teachers should establish reasonable classroom guidelines that are compatible with their teaching methods.

Con Argument 2: Prohibiting cell phones denies children and teenagers the opportunity to develop self-regulation and responsible digital habits.

In today's world, receiving a cell phone has become a significant rite of passage. It plays a crucial role in "the process of separation-individuation, during which [children and teenagers] are learning to manage social interactions with reduced support from their guardians and are forming their own sense of identity and self-worth; they are also looking for approval from their peers rather than from their parents," as noted by researchers. [44]

Resisting such developmental milestones with prohibitions is a futile, Sisyphean struggle that ultimately does not benefit the students. "We must instruct students on how to use cellphones and when it is responsible to do so. If we simply prohibit them, what are we genuinely achieving when they enter the workforce? How will they learn when it is suitable, where it is suitable, and how it is suitable to use them? The adolescent years are the time when we learn to navigate these things," stated Candice Breaux of West Baton Rouge Parish Schools in Louisiana. [45]

A report from UNESCO issued a warning that "students must be taught about the risks and opportunities associated with technology, acquire essential skills, and comprehend how to live both with and without technology. Insulating students from new and innovative technologies can place them at a disadvantage. It is crucial to approach these matters with a forward-looking perspective and be prepared to adapt and evolve as the world progresses." [16]

As technology continues to advance at a rapid pace, it is essential for students to learn how to use the immensely powerful device that is almost always in their possession responsibly. Richard Culatta, CEO of the International Society for Technology in Education, contended, "I want my children to be taught about proper technology use in an academic environment by a certified educator... who can guide them in finding a balance and ignite their curiosity. To shirk any part of this duty is to fail in one of the most vital responsibilities we have as parents and educators in a digital age." [46]

On another occasion, Culatta observed, "In some manner, we must assist young people in learning to utilize these tools proficiently, if we expect them to be successful in the future." [45]

Con Argument 3: Bans on cell phones may contribute to an unnecessary fear regarding the adoption of new technologies.

A sense of alarm nearly always arises with the introduction of new technology. When schools were first established, there was a fear that they would "overwhelm the children's minds and nervous systems with intricate and numerous subjects, and weaken their bodies through prolonged confinement," as stated in an 1883 medical journal. It was once believed that excessive academic pursuits by anyone would inevitably lead to mental illness. Calculators were predicted to undermine children's understanding of mathematical principles. And the discussion about whether the Internet will diminish intelligence continues to this day. [47][69]

Nevertheless, children and teenagers continue to attend school, use calculators (often on their phones), and utilize the Internet. Cell phones represent just the latest valuable technological advancement. Phones provide a vast arrayboth in number and qualityof digital educational applications that "enable students to stay focused for extended periods, advance their learning at a personalized rate, and free up crucial teacher time for small-group lessons and social-emotional development," as noted by former school principal and president of the educational technology firm Mrs. Wordsmith, Brandon Cardet-Hernandez. [48]

Educational applications enhance learning by making it more tailored, accessible, enjoyable, and adaptable, which helps to keep individual students more captivated. Students are able to collaborate on a global scale through apps and can use engaging, gamified learning resources such as Duolingo and Khan Academy. When used in conjunction with skilled teachers, these apps meet children and teenagers at their current level and support their path to success. [49]

Lastly, given the constraints of school budgets and the continuous advancement of technology, it is financially prudent to make use of a personal device that a great majority of students already possess and are fond of. [48]

Quotes Opposing a Ban

Educator Karen Gross expressed:

Considering the fires in Los Angeles, threats related to immigration, and other crises, I believe that establishing cell phone-free zones in schools will negatively affect the mental well-being of both students and parents (both of which are of concern).

Certainly, we can have containers at the front of the classroom where phones can be placed while class is in session (for all or part of the class period), but this is fundamentally different from complete bans and locked cell phone containers/devices at school entrances for holding student phones until they leave. These containers have a confining appearance and feel, and some also seem rather foreboding (like something from a prison). [54]

Brandon Cardet-Hernandez, a former school principal and current president of the educational technology company Mrs. Wordsmith, remarked:

We cannot ignore the powerful connection that exists between students and their phones. When I was an educator and later a principaland being from a different generationit required time for me to grasp this concept fully. Phones initially seemed to be a hindrance between myself and the students I was teaching. I had 10th-grade students who were reading at a 3rd-grade level, and I needed to figure out how to bridge the gap that I knew could have a profound impact on their future health and economic opportunities.

A realization occurred for me when I saw that a device could function as a tool and an entry point for learning, not merely as an obstacle. The current reality in classrooms is that students are familiar with having their phones presentand they have been taught to interact with technology with ease. For many children, particularly in districts that do not have the funds to supply alternative devices to students, the phone acts as a calculator, a translator, a small computer, and a gateway to information.

At the same time, educators have witnessed a surge in effective digital learning tools, many of which are based on apps. These tools enable students to remain engaged for longer durations, advance in their educational paths at their own speed, and allow teachers more valuable time for small-group instruction and social-emotional learning. [48]

Richard Culatta, the CEO of the International Society for Technology in Education, commented:

Prohibiting phones conveys the incorrect message.

The utility of a young person's phone is entirely contingent on what they are engaged in with it at any specific time. Digital activities can vary from significant (such as reading or connecting with family members who are far away) to trivial (like playing Candy Crush) or even detrimental (such as cyberbullying). I use the phrase "screen value" to define the degree of advantage a child gains from digital pursuits. When we prohibit phones, we are telling children that none of the activities on their device hold any genuine worth. This is precisely the opposite of the message we ought to be imparting. While insignificant, and certainly harmful, digital activities have no place in a school setting, a phone ban eliminates the chance to teach that there are also valuable and enriching activities that can be performed on those very same devices. [46]

High school student Olivia Faith stated:

Last year, when a report came in that there was a shooter at the high school, parents, students, and even the school staff were filled with fear. I had my phone with me because I was experiencing some health problems and had been given permission to have it. When the school was placed on lockdown, I sent a text to my stepdad, who is a police officer, and he informed me that several other schools had received the same report, and it was not authentic. Although the event was still very frightening, I received information from an outside source that helped to reassure me and those around me. Without my phone, I would not have been able to obtain this information. If more students had been allowed their phones that day, everyone would have felt a great deal more secure.

The primary justification for not allowing students to have their phones in class is the concern that they would be on them constantly and would be unable to learn due to the distraction. While this is a valid concern, it is not entirely accurate. If students were aware that they would have designated time to use their phones later, the majority of them would not use them during the times they were not permitted to. Naturally, there would be some students who would exploit the privilege of having their phones, but if they were to receive a written warning or detention at that point, it would be a fair consequence. [55]

Policies on School Cell Phone Use in U.S. States and Territories

The U.S. Department of Education has not established detailed regulations like cell phone bans, but in December 2024, the department did provide recommendations for schools and districts to formulate their own policies. This guidance is structured around three key principles:

  1. When device policies are collaboratively developed with a wide and representative group of stakeholdersincluding students, educators, parents, caregivers, and school administratorsthey are more likely to gain stakeholder support and have a higher chance of success.
  2. The likelihood of success is greater when there is widespread knowledge and a collective interest in both participating in and carrying out device policies. School administrators should consistently enforce these policies and maintain accountability for their execution. Educators should foster an effective classroom atmosphere and standards for teaching and learning that align with their school's device policy.
  3. Device policies ought to be complemented by a focus on digital citizenship both at school and at home, incorporating systematic, evidence-supported methods for instructing adults and children on how to ensure students are safe, healthy, and productive in online environments. [56]

State-level policies vary, ranging from mandatory statewide laws that every public school district must adhere to, to suggestions from the state that public schools can choose to implement. These can be categorized as follows:

  • Statewide Restrictions: Laws have been enacted and signed that regulate the use of cell phones in schools across the state.
  • Cell Phone Policy Mandated: The state mandates that every school district formulate and implement a cell phone policy tailored to the needs of the schools within its community. The state may offer guidance or model policies.
  • Incentive-Based State Policy: The state provides an incentive to school districts that adopt a cell phone policy, such as grant funding or cell phone pouches. The state might issue guidance or sample policies, or it may require the district to adopt a state-developed policy to be eligible for the program.
  • Cell Phone Policy Recommended: The state does not obligate school districts to enforce a cell phone policy but encourages them to do so. The state may provide guidance or model policies for consideration.

Only states that have established cell phone policies are listed below. The territories of the U.S. do not have extensive regulations regarding cell phone use in schools, although as of October 2024, Puerto Rico was in the process of considering such legislation. [57][58][59]

Delaware Districts are incentivized by the state to adopt a policy Governor John Carney signed Senate Bill 326 on June 30, 2024. This law provides $250,000 for a statewide pilot program to assess the effects of restricting cell phones in schools, mainly by supplying cell phone pouches to schools. The state Department of Education is required to assess the program and release a report by May 1, 2025.
Florida Statewide restriction Governor Ron DeSantis signed House Bill 379 on May 9, 2023. This law prohibits student use of cell phones and personal wireless devices from the first to the last bell of the school day, unless a teacher grants permission.
Georgia Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Brian Kemp signed House Bill 340 on May 9, 2025. The law requires school districts to establish bell-to-bell cell phone ban policies for K8 students by July 1, 2026. These policies must specify storage procedures for devices and provide clear guidelines for off-campus events. Schools must also outline how parents can contact their children during the school day and vice versa. Exceptions are provided for students with an IEP or a medical condition.
Idaho Districts are incentivized by the state to adopt a policy Governor Brad Little signed the Phone Free Learning Act executive order on October 31, 2024. This order allows the State Board of Education to give a single payment of $5,000 to school districts that implement cell phone policies that follow the Idaho Department of Educations policy guidelines.
Indiana Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Eric Holcomb enacted Senate Bill 185 on March 11, 2024. The law obligates school districts and charter schools to establish a policy that forbids students from using "any portable wireless device" (including tablets and laptops) during class time, except for educational purposes with a teacher's approval. Exceptions are made for students with an IEP, 504 plan, or other accommodation, in case of an emergency, and for managing a student's health needs.
Iowa Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Kim Reynolds signed House Bill 782 on April 30, 2025. This law mandates that school districts implement a policy restricting the use of cell phones and other personal devices during instructional hours. The policy must detail how and where cell phones will be kept, how parents can reach their children, and the process for parents to request that their children be allowed to keep their devices throughout the day. Exceptions are provided for students with an IEP or 504 plan. School districts are permitted to enact stricter regulations than those proposed by the state.
Kansas Districts are encouraged to adopt a policy In December 2024, the Kansas State Board of Education advised districts to implement a bell-to-bell policy that mandates cell phones and other personal electronic devices be stored away for the entire school day.
Kentucky Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Andy Beshear signed House Bill 208 into law on March 26, 2025. This law requires school districts to create a policy that, at a minimum, prohibits the use of cell phones and other personal devices during class time, unless a teacher permits it. District policies must also regulate the possession and use of personal devices at other times during the school day and at events off-campus. Exceptions are granted for students whose device use is allowed under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, or the Rehabilitation Act.
Louisiana Statewide restriction Governor Jeff Landry enacted Act 313 on May 28, 2024. The law stipulates that, starting with the 202425 school year, students are not permitted to have cell phones or other "telecommunication" devices on their person during school hours. These devices must be "properly stowed away." This act supersedes any more permissive district policies that might have allowed cell phone use during lunch or recess. Exceptions are made for students with an IEP, 504 plan, or other accommodation.
Minnesota Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Tim Walz signed Senate File 3567 on May 17, 2024, which mandates that school districts create a policy on cell phone possession and use by March 15, 2025. The law also instructs the Minnesota Elementary School Principals Association and the Minnesota Association of Secondary School Principals to compile a report on best practices.
Nebraska Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Jim Pillen signed Legislative Bill 140 into law on May 20, 2025. The law requires school districts to establish policies for cell phones and electronic devices by the start of the 202526 school year. These policies must forbid the use of cell phones and devices by students on school premises and at school-sponsored activities. The district may permit cell phone use for educational purposes. Exceptions are provided for emergencies and for students with a medical condition, an IEP, or a 504 plan.
New Mexico Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham signed Senate Bill 11 on April 10, 2025. This law mandates that the Public Education Department establish guidelines for minimum standards that school districts must implement by August 1, 2025. Students will be prohibited from using cell phones during class time, unless authorized by a teacher. A student may use such a device in case of emergencies, for health-related reasons, for accessibility needs, or if it is permitted as part of an IEP.
New York Districts are required to create and adopt a policy The Distraction-Free Schools initiative was incorporated into the fiscal year 2026 state budget. Governor Kathy Hochul advocated for the inclusion of a bell-to-bell prohibition on smartphones and smart devices. Students may be allowed to have phones that do not have Internet access. Exceptions are made for academic, translation, family care, medical, emergency, and accessibility needs, as well as for IEPs and 504 plans.
North Dakota Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Kelly Armstrong signed House Bill 1160 on April 25, 2025. This law requires school districts to prohibit cell phone use during instructional periods, with the choice to create a policy for cell phone use during non-instructional times of the school day. Exceptions are provided for medical reasons or if the student is permitted to have a device as part of an IEP or 504 plan.
Ohio Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Mike DeWine signed House Bill 250 into law on May 15, 2024. The law mandates that each school district formulate a cell phone policy that restricts usage during school hours. The Ohio Department of Education and Workforce provided a model policy on May 29, 2024.
Oklahoma Districts are encouraged to adopt a policy Governor Kevin Stitt signed an executive order on September 11, 2024, which challenges school district superintendents to create and implement cell phone policies by November 29, 2024. The governor's office will then compile a report on best practices, which is scheduled for release in early 2025.
Oregon Districts are encouraged to adopt a policy The Oregon Department of Education has provided non-mandatory guidance for school districts to implement restrictions on cell phone use.
Pennsylvania Districts are incentivized by the state to adopt a policy Governor Josh Shapiro signed Senate Bill 700 on July 11, 2024, which modifies an existing state grant program to permit funding for schools to acquire cell phone bags, provided the district implements a policy that limits cell phone use during the school day.
South Carolina Statewide restriction The South Carolina legislature enacted the General Appropriations Bill for 202425, H. 5100, which contains an "Anti-Bullying/School Safety" clause requiring school districts to prohibit cell phones during the school day to qualify for state aid for classrooms.
Following this, the South Carolina Department of Education introduced a "Free to Focus" policy that forbids student use of cell phones and other personal devices during school hours. Phones and other devices are to be kept in lockers or backpacks and can only be used for classroom instruction. Exceptions are granted for students with an IEP, 504 plan, or other educational accommodation. Students who are volunteer firefighters may request an exemption.
South Dakota Districts are encouraged to adopt a policy The South Dakota House of Representatives passed House Concurrent Resolution 6005 on February 4, 2025 (which the Senate agreed to on February 11, 2025). This non-binding resolution "strongly encourages" school districts to prohibit cell phones and other devices during instructional periods.
Tennessee Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Bill Lee signed House Bill 0932 into law on March 28, 2025. This law mandates that each school district establish a wireless device policy that forbids usage during class time, except with the teacher's permission. Exceptions are provided for emergencies, medical conditions, IEPs, and 504 plans.
Utah Statewide restriction Governor Spencer Cox signed Senate Bill 178 on March 25, 2025. This law prohibits the use of cell phones and other devices during class time. Exceptions are made for emergencies, use of the SafeUT crisis line (which provides a connection for people in crisis to licensed counselors), medical necessities, and for students with an IEP or 504 plan.
Virginia Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Glenn Youngkin signed Executive Order 33, which directs the state's Department of Education to issue model phone-free policies by August 15, 2024, and sets a January 2025 deadline for schools to implement a phone-free policy. The department has provided sample policies for elementary, middle, and high school students.
Washington Districts are encouraged to adopt a policy The state has suggested that districts formulate their own cell phone policies to begin with the 202526 school year.
The Washington Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction (OSPI) distributed a memo on August 28, 2024, advising limits on cell phone use. Schools were urged to create policies that restrict cell phone usage by the start of the 202526 school year, using the OSPI guidance.
West Virginia Districts are required to create and adopt a policy Governor Patrick Morrisey signed House Bill 2003 on April 29, 2025. This law mandates that county boards of education create and enforce cell phone policies by the beginning of the 202526 school year. These policies must, at a minimum, prohibit cell phones and other devices during instructional periods. County boards have the option to ban cell phones from school property entirely. Exceptions are provided for medical needs and for students with an IEP or 504 plan.

International School Cell Phone Policies

A UNESCO report indicates that nearly one out of every four countries in the world has prohibited cell phones in schools, with such bans being more prevalent in the nations of Central and South Asia. It should be noted that the following table might not encompass all countries, territories, and dependencies that have a national policy on cell phone use in schools. [16]
