Why Smartphones Should Be Removed From K-12 Classrooms

The presence of smartphones and other mobile devices conflicts with the fundamental goals of education.

By Heath Madom

Published on June 26, 2023

During the previous autumn, a significant challenge emerged for me and my fellow educators: the widespread use of smartphones in our classes had become uncontrolled, and we found it difficult to manage.

This issue was not characterized by students openly browsing social media or engaging in games like Clash Royale. The usage was more subtle, involving students checking their devices in frequent, brief intervals to send a quick message, view a notification, or sometimes watch a short TikTok video. However, these interruptions occurred numerous times throughout a single class session, and the cumulative distraction severely compromised the students' capacity to concentrate on their schoolwork.

To address this challenge, our team collectively implemented a strict zero-tolerance policy for smartphones at the beginning of the spring term. Students were given a clear warning at the commencement of each class, after which any visible phone was confiscated for the remainder of the period. We meticulously recorded infractions for each student across all our classes, and any student whose phone was taken more than once was reported to the school administration for further action. Remarkably, within just a few days of introducing this new rule, smartphone use in our classrooms virtually disappeared, which felt like an incredible success.

This anecdote is shared not to propose it as a universally scalable solution, but rather to demonstrate the significant problem that phones represent in educational settings now. The issue has become so pronounced that I am convinced the time has come to prohibit them from schools entirely. Several high schools have already taken this exact step, a decision that is entirely understandable. The functionality of smartphones is in direct opposition to the core purpose of an educational environment. It is impossible to maintain focus on other tasks while simultaneously engaged with a phone.

A reasonable counterargument might be to question why all schools don't simply enforce strict no-usage policies, similar to the one my colleagues and I implemented. However, this approach is not a comprehensive solution for two primary reasons.

The first reason is that policing smartphone usage often results in confrontational power struggles that can negatively impact the relationship between teachers and students. Students, much like adults, have a strong attachment to their mobile devices; therefore, even a successful confiscation can potentially damage the rapport with the student. Throughout my teaching career, nearly every significant conflict I have encountered with students has revolved around phone use. I have experienced students leaving the classroom in anger or even using profane language when I have insisted they surrender their device. This type of conflict ultimately generates more issues than it resolves.

The second, and more critical, point is that the responsibility for policing smartphone use should not fall on teachers to begin with. The design of smartphones and social media applications is intentionally engineered to monopolize a user's attention. It is a widely acknowledged fact, yet our society continues to permit—and in many ways, encourage—high school students to carry these powerful distraction devices onto school grounds daily. One should consider the multitude of factors that result in a student having a phone in class: the vast financial investments in software and hardware, the extensive research aimed at maximizing user engagement, and the personal decision of parents to provide their child with a phone. These elements combine to implicitly support students bringing phones to school. It is therefore unreasonable to expect individual educators to single-handedly prevent teenagers from using these highly engaging devices.

Rather than imposing this responsibility on individual educators, a cultural shift within schools is necessary, making the presence of a smartphone in a classroom as unacceptable as smoking would be; it simply would not be done. The most effective method to start this transformation would be for the California Department of Education to release official policy guidance declaring that student access to phones should be prohibited during school hours, which effectively means banning phones from classrooms. For students who must bring a phone to school, a system could be implemented where they deposit their device at the main office before their first class, utilizing options like phone lockers. Such a measure would relieve teachers and administrators from the duty of enforcing separate, individual bans. A statewide policy would instead establish a clear and universal prohibition for both students and parents.

It is important to acknowledge that a complete ban has potential disadvantages, chief among them being the elimination of structured, supervised environments where adolescents can learn to cultivate a healthy relationship with their phones. Since screens and mobile technology are an integral part of modern life, a valid argument exists that educators have a duty to instruct students on how to manage the constant stream of notifications and the allure of digital distraction. Furthermore, there are occasional instances where smartphones can serve as valuable educational tools in a lesson, using applications such as Kahoot or Poll Everywhere. Lastly, a significant concern for some parents may be that a ban could impede their ability to contact their child in an emergency situation.

Although I am receptive to these counterarguments, my firsthand experience as an educator observing how phones decimate student focus leads me to conclude that these points are not compelling enough. For emergency communications, every school office and classroom I am familiar with is equipped with a landline phone. More importantly, our fundamental responsibility as educators is to establish environments conducive to learning that serve the best interests of our students. The presence of smartphones directly conflicts with both of these objectives, not to mention the documented risks they pose to adolescent mental health. Therefore, if our true priority is the well-being and academic success of students, it is imperative for California to spearhead the movement to remove phones from K-12 learning spaces.

Heath Madom is a humanities educator at Life Academy of Health and Bioscience, part of the Oakland Unified School District. Following a brief trial with a basic, non-smart phone, he is now actively working to reduce his personal smartphone use with a tool called a kSafe.

Rob Foster

As a retired middle school science teacher, I can completely appreciate the inclination to implement a ban. However, I chose a different path. I developed a comprehensive contract governing the use of what started as cell phones but eventually included smartphones, tablets, and wearable technology like the Apple Watch. The contract established rules for when, where, and how these devices could be utilized. For example, they could be used for collecting data during science experiments, listening to music during independent work, and conducting research. The terms of use stipulated requirements such as maintaining a certain minimum GPA, keeping the device flat on the desk where I could see it, prohibiting the sharing of earbuds, and not taking pictures of other students during data collection, among other things. Both parents and students were required to sign the contract. For the most part, this system was effective. By replacing a combative, policing dynamic around electronic devices with one that leveraged them as classroom tools, I saw a significant positive change. I would not claim that misuse was eliminated entirely, but it decreased by a very substantial margin. My students understood when they were permitted to use their devices, they would ask for permission to ensure it was appropriate, and they adhered to the rules they had agreed to. While our children are adept at entertaining themselves with smartphones, very few, in my observation, knew how to use the device as a learning tool. They are digital natives, and these devices are like a second nature to them. I hold the belief that we have an obligation to teach them the proper way, time, and place to use them. This was my personal experience in a middle school classroom setting. Many other teachers at my school and within the district adopted my contract, and to my knowledge, they continue to use it.
