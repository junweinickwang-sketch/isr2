A Balanced View of Controversial Issues

The Arguments For and Against Banning Cellphones in Schools

Published: February 01, 2024
Topic: Education

A significant debate is expanding across the nation concerning whether cellphones should be prohibited in schools. From one perspective, numerous educators contend that mobile phones serve as a barrier to learning and also contribute to elevated stress levels and mental health problems among the student population. Conversely, on the opposing side of this issue, certain parents have voiced worries about safety if their children are unable to use their cellphones throughout the school day. This discussion appears to be intensifying as an increasing number of school-aged children now possess smartphones.

For instance, a survey conducted last year by the Pew Research Center revealed that 95 percent of teenagers in the United States have access to a mobile phone. That same research also discovered that 46 percent of the teens surveyed indicated they are connected to the internet on an "almost constant" basis. In the previous year, Florida passed legislation to limit student cellphone usage during instructional periods, and following the law's implementation last July, several school districts within the state have enacted even stricter measures, such as forbidding cellphone use for the duration of the school day.

To date, the outcomes of these restrictions have generated predominantly positive feedback from both teachers and school administrators. "I am not certain... what factors led to the creation of that rule, but I can share that its impact on a broad scale has been exceptionally beneficial for [students'] mental well-being, based on anecdotal evidence," stated Heather Kreider, principal of Edgewater High School in Florida's Orange County school district, in an interview with Education Week. "Our students demonstrate significantly higher levels of engagement.

The disinterest we observed in them over the past one to two years appears to have diminished. They appear to be reawakening to the social experience of school.” Beyond increased social interaction during school hours, some administrators also note that curbing or prohibiting phone usage can aid students' socio-emotional growth. As an illustration, it is quite common for students to turn to their phones as a refuge after experiencing something unpleasant or difficult at school, like receiving a poor grade on an examination.

That student might then retreat to a restroom to text their mother as a way to cope with the discomfort, instead of learning to manage it independently, as explained by Roni Cohen-Sandler, a clinical psychologist and the author of the book "Anything But My Phone, Mom!". "They are, in a way, offloading all of this onto their mothers and are not processing these feelings on their own," Cohen-Sandler communicated to The Hill.

“They are failing to build a tolerance for frustration. They are not really challenging themselves to cultivate coping mechanisms for handling difficult emotions, nor are they resolving issues by utilizing the resources accessible to them within the school environment.” Furthermore, research indicates a correlation between cellphone prohibitions and improved academic test scores.

A study carried out in Australia, for example, discovered that the test scores of students aged 16 and older increased by 6.4 percent after their schools implemented smartphone bans. Nevertheless, not all individuals support the idea of restricting or prohibiting cellphone usage throughout the school day. Certain students and parents have expressed apprehension about the inability to communicate during the day, particularly in emergency situations or when a student needs to contact a parent.

Other opponents contend that enforcing rules on cellphone use in schools can negatively impact the educational environment and damage relationships between students and teachers. "In my opinion, school-wide bans are counterproductive," stated Christopher Klein, a specialist in learning achievement from Montgomery County, Maryland, to the NEAToday publication of the National Education Association. "We must exercise caution to avoid eroding the relationships between teachers and students and to prevent our schools from feeling more like correctional facilities."

Moreover, certain advocates for technology assert that complete prohibitions fail to educate children on how to use technology in a responsible or balanced manner. "A significantly more effective strategy is to ensure the creation of a school culture that promotes healthy technology use," Richard Culatta, the CEO of the International Society for Technology in Education, explained to The Hill. "It is imperative that we teach these skills. They are fundamentally essential. No one in the world could argue that proficiency in healthy technology habits is unnecessary for future employment and overall life satisfaction. Therefore, why would we possibly neglect to teach these skills that we recognize as being so critically important?"

Beyond Florida, school districts throughout the nation have experimented with a variety of methods for limiting or banning smartphones in schools, which include enforcing penalties like suspension and using special pouches that secure phones during the day. The discussion regarding the efficacy of these restrictions is expected to persist as educational districts test different strategies and as additional research becomes available.

Educators, parents, and students will likely continue to hold conflicting views on this matter.
