    Prohibitions on Cellphones Are Growing More Frequent in California Schools

    Studies indicate that immoderate smartphone usage is associated with dependency and other negative effects

    Diana Lambert, Mallika Seshadri, And Lasherica Thornton

    August 21, 2024

    This year, throughout California and the entire United States, new policies are being implemented to prohibit or limit student cellphone usage at schools, aiming to reduce bullying, minimize classroom disruptions, and combat device addiction.

    “This is currently part of the cultural conversation, and a movement toward cellphone limitation is evident,” stated Troy Flint, a representative for the California School Boards Association. “The matter is receiving more critical examination at this time than it has in the past.”

    Among the educational districts in California that are beginning the academic year with new cellphone limitations on their properties are Lincoln Unified in Stockton, Santa Barbara Unified, San Francisco Unified, Roseville City, and Folsom Cordova Unified located near Sacramento.

    Across California, the specific nature of cellphone limitations varies, contingent upon the policies of the school district, the individual school, or even the rules of a particular teacher. At certain schools, pupils must place their phones into a secure electronic pouch upon entering the campus or a classroom, which can be opened exclusively by staff using a specialized magnet. Alternatively, some schools mandate that cellphones be powered down and stored inside classroom lockers. The most frequent approach involves requesting that students switch off their devices and keep them in their backpacks or pockets throughout the duration of the class.

    Leaders of California's school districts received encouragement from Governor Gavin Newsom last week, as he called on them to promptly enact measures to limit cellphone use during the current academic year. Governor Newsom also reminded these school administrators that legislation passed in 2019 grants them the power to control smartphone usage during school operational hours.

    “Immoderate smartphone use among young individuals is correlated with heightened anxiety, depression, and additional mental health challenges,” Governor Newsom communicated in a letter to school officials on August 14.

    Furthermore, California legislators are reviewing proposed laws that would limit student cellphone activity on all public school grounds, a requirement that has already been adopted by at least five other states. In the absence of a statewide directive, the responsibility to create and enforce such a policy falls to individual districts, schools, or teachers.

    Officials from San Diego Unified have signaled that they are examining the matter, whereas Los Angeles Unified (LAUSD), which is the state's most populous school district, is completing a policy designed to prohibit student use of cellphones and social media. This new regulation is scheduled to be implemented in January.

    “Children are no longer afforded the chance to simply be children,” remarked Nick Melvoin, the LAUSD school board member who drafted the resolution that prompted the policy. “It is my hope that this resolution will not only improve students' focus in the classroom but also provide them an opportunity to interact and connect more meaningfully with one another — and to just be kids.”

    Melvoin praised Governor Newsom for motivating other districts to adopt similar measures.

    “I have personally witnessed the beneficial outcomes at schools that have already established a phone-free school policy, and I am eager to observe the positive impacts of this policy as it is implemented across the district next semester,” Melvoin stated.

    However, these policies have encountered resistance from a number of parents who express concern about being unable to contact their children during emergency situations.

    “Certain parents and families believe the cellphone is vital for communication in the event of a natural disaster, a school-related emergency, or a school shooting,” explained Flint from the CSBA. “Additionally, some individuals use it for less critical, yet still significant purposes, such as overseeing their children’s necessary medication schedules. Some families with students who have disabilities appreciate having another method of communication with their children at school.”

    The problem of cellphone addiction

    The movement for school cellphone prohibitions gained significant national traction in May after Surgeon General Vivek Murthy published an advisory. This advisory urged policymakers, technology firms, researchers, and families to work towards reducing the negative impacts of social media and to foster safer, more wholesome online spaces to safeguard children.

    Dr. Murthy noted that a substantial body of evidence is emerging which links social media engagement to adverse effects on the mental health of young people, also pointing out that 95% of children aged 13 to 17 use a minimum of one social media platform, with over one-third engaging with social media persistently.

    Santa Barbara Unified has prioritized mental health in its approach to cellphone usage on its campuses. The "Off and Away" policy mandates that cellphones must be turned off and stored away within classrooms and any other campus area designated for learning, as stated by Assistant Superintendent ShaKenya Edison.

    The repercussions for failing to adhere to the policy can vary from requiring students and their parents to attend meetings with school personnel, to the confiscation of the phones. If deemed necessary, students might also be directed to counseling or therapeutic services, Edison mentioned.

    “A key point that the (planning) committee was adamant about — and we had medical doctors and psychologists on our committee as well — is the necessity to approach cellphone usage as an addiction rather than an act of defiance,” Edison stated. “Thus, the true goal is to address the fundamental issue of phone dependency.”

    Students developed a greater dependence on cellphones and smartwatches throughout the Covid pandemic, as these devices were their sole means of staying connected with their social networks, Edison remarked. She added that students might use their phones to manage classroom-related anxiety or as a coping mechanism when facing academic difficulties.

    According to researchers from the University of San Francisco, children in the U.S. aged 12 to 13 saw their non-academic screen time double, increasing from 3.8 hours per day to 7.7 hours daily, while school campuses were shut down during the pandemic.

    Jason Nagata, an associate professor of pediatrics at the University of California San Francisco, identified several warning signs of smartphone addiction in students. These include feeling distressed at the prospect of being without their phone, frequently thinking about their device when not using it, immediately stopping their current activity to respond to a phone notification, or engaging in arguments with others due to their phone usage.

    Santa Barbara Unified is confronting the issue of cellphone addiction both within and beyond the school setting. In addition to involving parents in the program's development, the district provides them with resources on its website about supervising social media and selecting age-appropriate applications.

    “We are met with gratitude from parents who tell us, ‘We appreciate you addressing this issue. I am attempting to manage it at home, and I am unsure how to handle the dependency. So, thank you for at least managing it on the school premises,’” Edison shared.

    Students exhibit greater focus without phones

    Andrea Blair-Simon reports that the prohibition of cellphone use within the Folsom Cordova Unified School District has enabled her eighth-grade daughter, Laila, to concentrate fully on her academic work in class and to engage socially with her peers during breaks and lunchtime. She noted that she had previously observed her daughter and her friends sitting together while texting each other instead of engaging in conversation.

    “I am a strong supporter of the cellphone policy,” Blair-Simon expressed. “I believe it is advantageous for the students and for the teachers. My point is not to prohibit having a cellphone, but rather that it is not needed during school hours. Before or after school, you are free to do as you please. That is your own life and your own time. However, when you are under a teacher’s supervision — during school time — and utilizing school resources, it is important to listen to your teacher.”

    The no-phone regulations also help to reduce online bullying, Blair-Simon added. She explained that actions such as sharing unflattering photos accompanied by cruel remarks can be detrimental to a child’s self-esteem.

    Following last year’s update to the cellphone policy, Folsom Cordova Unified has ceased to allow students from transitional kindergarten to eighth grade to use cellphones, smartwatches, or any other mobile communication devices on campus at any time during the school day. High school students are prohibited from using them inside their classrooms.

    During the previous year, Laila and her peers were mandated to use a lockable Yondr Pouch, a device that lets students retain their phones but prevents access until a teacher or school official unlocks it. This year, rather than using the pouches, students are instructed to power down their phones and store them away.

    “This year, there are no preliminary warnings; you are immediately sent to the office,” Laila explained. “They now have a small locker in the office, a kind of phone locker, where your phone must be secured until the end of the day if you are caught with it.”

    Although Laila would prefer to have access to her phone during lunch or between classes, she concedes that since the ban was implemented, students are more attentive and engage in more conversation with each other during their breaks.

    Policies lead to an improved school atmosphere

    Keith Carames, a drama teacher, notes a significant positive change in the culture and atmosphere at James Lick Middle School in San Francisco since the school implemented a policy requiring students to secure their phones in a Yondr Pouch upon arrival each day.

    “There has been a notable reduction in the constant buzzing and other distractions,” Carames observed. “We have also seen a substantial drop in instances of digital bullying.”

    The school operates under the jurisdiction of San Francisco Unified, which mandates that cellphones, smartwatches, and similar mobile devices must be switched off and stored away during class sessions and the time between them.

    James Lick Middle School enforces its own, more stringent policy, which mandates that students must have a school-issued lockable pouch with them when they arrive on campus, regardless of whether it contains a phone. If a student is without their pouch, their phone will be taken. Should a student's phone be found outside the pouch during school hours, security is summoned to confiscate it, Carames detailed.

    Certain districts within the state that lack a comprehensive, district-wide cellphone prohibition grant individual schools the autonomy to establish their own regulations regarding cellphone usage on their premises.

    Fresno Unified operates under a two-decade-old policy that forbids students from using phones in ways that are inappropriate and disruptive, such as violating someone's privacy, cheating on assessments, or engaging in ridicule or shaming. Students who breach this policy may face confiscation of their phones, suspension, or even expulsion.

    The board's policy establishes the “baseline standard” for the district, as Fresno Unified spokesperson A.J. Kato communicated to EdSource on Wednesday. Every school has the authority to decide how this policy is carried out on its own campus and possesses the flexibility to implement measures that are stricter than what the policy requires.

    In 2022, Bullard High within the Fresno Unified district adopted the Yondr Pouch system to establish a phone-free environment on campus, as detailed by The Fresno Bee. Students are required to secure their phones within these pouches for the entire school day, including the lunch period. Following the 2022-23 academic year, the inaugural year with the pouches, officials at Bullard High attributed their 17% gain in English proficiency to this new restriction, The Bee reported.

    Educators are largely in favor of restrictions

    According to a 2023 Pew Research report, teachers across the country identify cellphones as a primary source of distraction for students in the classroom. One-third of public K-12 teachers who participated in the survey described cellphones as a significant issue, with an additional 20% labeling them a minor issue. Nearly 75% of the high school teachers surveyed reported that phones are a major distraction for their students, in contrast to 33% of middle school teachers and only 6% of elementary school teachers who said the same.

    Disruptions caused by cellphones in the classroom have been a persistent subject of discussion for educators and administrators during staff meetings in the Roseville City School District, according to school board member Jonathan Zachreson.

    Zachreson mentioned that some teachers in the district carried out a casual experiment by having students track the number of alerts they received on their phones during a class period. The educators found that the students who received the highest number of alerts were also the ones achieving lower academic results compared to their peers.

    The K-8 district, located near Sacramento, has implemented a new cellphone policy this year with the goal of reducing classroom disturbances and behavioral issues. This policy mandates that students must power off and store away their cellphones, personal tablets, Bluetooth headphones, or smartwatches throughout the school day.

    The district’s elementary schools previously had a no-phone rule, but its application was inconsistent across the district, Zachreson noted. Consequently, the district opted to establish a consistent policy and apply it across all grade levels.

    Even in the absence of district-wide mandates, certain teachers have independently prohibited phones within their own classrooms. Nicolle Fefferman, a veteran educator in the LAUSD and a co-founder of the Facebook group Parents Supporting Teachers, is among them. Fefferman stated that when cellphones are not put away, it becomes difficult for teachers to effectively "police" their usage.

    “I used to explain to my students: ‘I have such a limited amount of time with you each day that I am quite selfish. I am quite greedy,’” Fefferman recalled. “‘I require every moment of your focus for the tasks we are undertaking together in this classroom.’”

    United Teachers Los Angeles, the union that represents over 35,000 educators throughout LAUSD, endorses the board's move to establish a policy applicable to the entire district.

    “For these regulations to be successful, robust cooperation is crucial,” Gina Gray, an English teacher at an LAUSD middle school, conveyed to EdSource in a statement representing the union.

    “Administrators in the school district need to collaborate closely with both educators and parents to enact these modifications,” Gray stated. “Educators are deeply invested in the welfare of our students, and it is important that their families are part of the decision-making process regarding alterations to our school environments.”

    David Goldberg, President of the California Teachers Association, concurs: “Our union has been a proponent of enhancing school settings and limiting the use of smartphones on school grounds,” he declared in a statement. “As educators, our constant aim is to assist our students in achieving their utmost potential, and we are influenced by the data, the feedback from our students and their families, and our personal observations which indicate that smartphones can serve as a distraction and be detrimental to the mental health of students.”

    Prohibitions are gaining nationwide traction

    California could soon align with Florida, Indiana, Louisiana, South Carolina, and Ohio by enacting legislation that either prohibits or limits the use of cellphones on public school properties.

    While current California law permits districts to limit cellphone use on campus, it does not mandate them to do so. This could be altered if a proposed bill currently navigating the Legislature is enacted. Assembly Bill 3216 would mandate that school districts formulate a policy to either restrict or forbid smartphone use by students. The bill was approved by the Senate Appropriations Committee last week and is anticipated to reach the governor's desk for final authorization, as reported by School Services, an education consulting firm.

    A separate legislative measure, Senate Bill 1283, would authorize, but not compel, districts to restrict students' engagement with social media during their time on campus. It is anticipated that this bill will be presented for a vote on the Assembly floor within this month.

    These proposed bills enjoy support from both political parties.

    “Josh Hoover, a Republican, is the one advancing this legislation (Assembly Bill 3216),” Zachreson noted. “Gavin Newsom is urging school districts to move forward. You also have Ron DeSantis and an Arkansas governor taking similar actions. Frankly, when you find Gavin Newsom and Ron DeSantis agreeing on an issue, I believe you have a proposition that is likely to succeed.”
