Implementing School Cellphone Policies with a Community-First Strategy

By Jamie N. and Christine Elgersma | Published on June 5, 2024

Utilize these recommendations to identify the most suitable approach for your educational community.

The topic of cellphone bans is currently prominent across various platforms, appearing in news reports, legislative agendas, and school board meetings. Legislation is being enacted at the state level that mandates specific actions for certain districts, leaving schools to navigate the practical implementation challenges. This trend is driven by a clear cause: educators are managing learning environments where students are often engaged in creating TikTok videos, browsing social media, and playing games instead of focusing on their lessons. Our own research indicates that 53 percent of tweens, aged 8 to 12, and 69 percent of teens, aged 13 to 18, have stated that social media usage serves as a distraction from their academic responsibilities. For many observers, implementing phone bans in schools appears to be an obvious solution. However, the process of creating and enforcing these prohibitions is not always straightforward.

A complete prohibition on cellphones is a perfectly feasible option for certain school communities. Conversely, in a large high school with 3,000 students, it can be almost impossible to find the resources needed to secure each phone while also permitting access for diverse schedules and requirements, all while managing parental safety concerns. Such logistical challenges are often not addressed by legislative mandates, placing the burden of finding solutions on school administrators and teachers.

Ultimately, any phone policy a school adopts must be practical for the specific community it supports, and the institution must have the necessary backing to implement it effectively.

Below are our suggestions for determining a workable policy for your community and for putting that plan into action:

Involve the community

When all stakeholders participate in the development and execution of the policy, it results in greater acceptance and backing. Provide numerous accessible opportunities for families, educators, and students to offer their input, such as through in-person meetings, surveys, or designated office hours. While complete consensus is rare, listening to the perspectives of all affected groups is essential for a successful outcome. After gathering feedback, it is crucial to inform those who did not participate about the steps taken during the policy's creation. You could frame this as an exciting 'relaunch' to ensure the new policy receives adequate attention. In the end, the key is to listen attentively and then disseminate the information broadly.

Prioritize and set realistic goals

After you have collected information and received feedback, the next step is to establish priorities. Considering core priorities like learning, social connection, safety, and a positive school climate, you must determine how personal devices align with these goals. Is your school currently facing significant challenges with violence or classroom management? Furthermore, what are the considerations regarding equity?

The essential point is this: when educators are expected to enforce device policies without reliable support or consequences, they are faced with an unmanageable responsibility.

For example, at the previously mentioned 3,000-student high school, stakeholders could conclude that while cellphones are disruptive in class, some students require them for work or transportation, leading to a policy that permits phones on campus but not within classrooms, which is viable if the entire community collaborates on enforcement. In a similar vein, the policy will fail if a significant number of families oppose its enforcement by the school. Obstacles are probable, but if the policy is developed through a solid process, communicated clearly, and enforced consistently, the positive outcomes will become evident over time.

Consider research and developmentally appropriate practices

Utilize established research on cellphone use in schools, along with any local data you can collect, to form a basis for policy changes. A solid starting point is to review studies on the impacts of cellphone usage and to consider guidelines from reputable organizations. In this context, it is vital to account for the specific ages and developmental needs of your students, recognizing that an elementary student's capacity for self-regulation is different from that of a teenager.

Arguably, the most fundamental aspect is that students must acquire the skills to manage their own personal devices and usage habits.

Make digital citizenship core to student culture

To be candid, it is widely acknowledged that many adults find it difficult to cope with the distracting and compulsive nature of cellphone use. Because students possess fewer developmental self-regulation skills than adults, effecting change in their relationship with devices requires our active participation as part of a broader solution. Establishing a culture of digital citizenship involves integrating these principles into everyday school activities and providing families with strategies to implement at home. Although digital citizenship is often equated with internet safety and anti-cyberbullying initiatives, it encompasses a much wider range of concepts necessary for students to understand device usage comprehensively. Encouraging children to consider the reasons they reach for their phones, the difficulty in setting them aside, and the personal impact of their device habits are all crucial components in developing stronger self-regulation. This process also requires modeling appropriate behavior; therefore, even though school staff may have legitimate reasons to use their phones, demonstrating responsible use is critically important.

We offer a vast collection of free resources for both students and teachers, suitable for nearly any situation, and we are continually developing new materials to share. Additionally, we are collaborating with legislators and families, as it is essential for everyone—not just educational institutions—to work collectively to promote children's well-being. Consequently, regardless of whether you choose a full ban, no ban, or a hybrid policy, adopting a holistic, community-wide strategy with digital citizenship as its foundation represents a potent path to success.
