Are School Cellphone Bans Effective? Educators Provide Their Perspectives

By Arianna Prothero — November 21, 2023

iStock / Getty Images Plus

Educational institutions are being swiftly transformed by technology, encompassing artificial intelligence, one-to-one computing initiatives, learning management systems, and the large volumes of student performance data these tools generate.

However, if you consult with educators, they will inform you that the ever-present cellphone is the technology that causes the greatest disruption to classrooms and school settings.

According to Common Sense Media, a minimum of 88 percent of teenagers possess their own smartphone, and 43 percent of children aged 8 to 12 also own a personal smartphone.

Based on an October survey from the EdWeek Research Center, which consistently polls American educators on educational matters, roughly one in ten educators report a ban on cellphones in their schools, whereas 22 percent advocate for a campus-wide prohibition.

Slightly more than 200 educators responded to an open-ended question by sharing their viewpoints and expressing rising concerns about cellphones; some likened students' phone usage to an addiction, while others recounted situations where students reacted with panic when their devices were confiscated.

A high school teacher in Washington state stated, "The problem of high school students using their cellphones during instructional time is enormous." This educator added, "A large number of them are perpetually seeking the dopamine release that comes from checking their phones, texting, or viewing YouTube, and as a result, they have lost the capacity for any kind of sustained focus."

A high school educator from Pennsylvania commented, "The ongoing battle concerning cellphones is mentally and physically tiring for teachers."

"Pupils using their cellphones are physically in the room, yet they are not mentally engaged," remarked a third high school instructor from Iowa, who also noted, "This currently represents the single most significant danger to a quality education at our school."

These challenges are contributing to an increasing level of interest from educators, and also from some parents, in prohibiting cellphones within school environments.

So, is the prohibition of cellphones in classrooms or on school grounds effective? What are the firsthand observations and experiences of educators in schools where such bans exist? The feedback received indicates that a significant number of educators feel that these bans have led to better classroom and school atmospheres, but the success is highly dependent on the specifics of how the ban is put into practice.

Positive Outcomes of Implementing Cellphone Bans in Schools

“Our school recently prohibited cellphones. Before, their use was permitted during passing periods and lunch. Nevertheless, their presence had begun to dominate instructional periods. Pupils would instinctively reach for their phones, requiring teachers to dedicate as much effort to redirecting behavior as they did to teaching, or alternatively, to engage in a power struggle over confiscation.”

—District leader, Missouri

“Our district has recently put a no-cellphone policy into effect, and the result has been life-altering. For the most part, the students are now engaged with their schoolwork, communicating with each other, and staying out of trouble.”

—High school teacher, Ohio

“We have required students to place their powered-down phones into a magnetically sealed pouch. This has been instrumental in reducing the distraction that phones cause. I once believed our role was to teach responsible usage, but I now see that as an impossibility... it would be akin to attempting to teach someone to use heroin responsibly. My preference now is for them to have absolutely no access to their phones.”

—Middle school teacher, Wisconsin

“For our middle school, we have established a 'no cellphones' policy during the school day and have noticed enhancements in student interactions, particularly during lunchtime with board game activities and in the gymnasium.”

—Middle school teacher, Indiana

“This marks the first year our school has enforced a cellphone policy. I have consistently maintained one in my own classroom, and I am convinced that my students have always demonstrated much greater focus. I encountered no negative interactions with students due to having a policy when other teachers did not. The children simply adjusted to my established expectations.”

—High school teacher, Minnesota

“I believe that students should not possess a cellphone at any time during the standard school day. Our district initiated a no-cellphone policy this year, and the outcome has been exclusively positive. Students are demonstrating more attentiveness in classes, and there is significantly less online bullying compared to past years when cellphones were permitted.”

—Middle school teacher, Tennessee

“In my classes, I do not permit cellphones. Students are required to put them in the calculator caddy and take a calculator instead. They dislike it intensely; they want their phones with them constantly. By the end of the year, I've had students reluctantly confess that being without their phones aided their concentration on their assignments.”

—High school teacher, Pennsylvania

“Last school year, cellphones presented a major distraction in my classrooms. The modifications implemented by the district were essential and are proving beneficial. We are already observing a notable difference in both student engagement and academic performance.”

—High school teacher, North Carolina

“My school has instituted a school-wide no-cellphone policy during class time, unless it is approved by the teacher. We utilize phone vaults, and students are accustomed to their use. Now that the routine is established among students, I no longer have to concern myself with cellphone usage. They are aware that phones are not allowed, they are stored in a pocket during class, and the entire school is united in this approach, which is fantastic!”

—High school teacher, Idaho

Challenges and Reasons Why School Cellphone Bans May Fail

“Our school maintains a policy against cellphones in academic classrooms; however, its enforcement is highly inconsistent. This inconsistency creates challenges for me when trying to uphold a strict policy in my own classroom. Furthermore, some parents contest the policy, insisting their children should have constant access to their phones.”

—High school teacher, Illinois

“Our school administration will not enforce a cellphone policy across the entire building but instead expects each class to create an individual plan. The teaching staff perceives a schoolwide phone policy as an administrative responsibility. Consequently, we lack any enforceable cellphone policy, which is directly hindering students' academic progress.”

—High school teacher, Washington state

“Phones are impossible to police unless the school administration enforces a stringent no-cellphone-use policy that includes clear consequences. No one wants to handle a student's property, and students frequently ignore requests to put their phones away. It is unfeasible for a teacher to compete with the allure and addictive nature of a cellphone. The device is perpetually alerting them with pings, chimes, and notifications that demand their attention.”

—Middle school teacher, New York

“Cellphones are the root of a multitude of issues, both inside and outside the classroom. There is no support from parents to adhere to the district's policy. In fact, parents will phone and text their child even with the knowledge that they are in class. The sharing of test answers, recording of fights and conflicts, and bullying are also significant problems that could be readily addressed by banning cellphones on campus. Regrettably, I do not foresee this ever being realized through consistent district enforcement.”

—High school teacher, West Virginia

“Our schedule is exceedingly complex and features 90-minute blocks that are both physically and mentally taxing. The new no-phone policy, while new, introduces an additional layer of tension between my students and me.”

—High school teacher, Virginia

“Regardless of the official school rules, they are not consistently enforced by every teacher. To curb cellphone usage during class, I have established a cellphone turn-in incentive program within my own classroom.”

—High school teacher, Illinois

Alternative Strategies When a Full Cellphone Ban Isn't Feasible:

“I recently assigned my 9th-grade students a journal entry about a societal change they'd like to see to avoid outcomes depicted in the dystopian literature we've studied. A majority of them confessed a belief that everyone should reduce their phone usage. They recognize that endlessly scrolling through TikTok and Instagram is unproductive and negatively affects their mood, yet they also acknowledge feeling addicted to it. … When I discuss cellphone habits with students, I emphasize that this isn't solely a teenage issue by sharing how my own phone's proximity hinders my concentration during lesson planning or grading. By sharing my personal struggles and observations, I find they are more inclined to comply with my classroom rule of placing their phones in a designated slot for the class period. My own daughter even admitted that while she was initially annoyed, she also felt a sense of relief when her teachers mandated phone organizers, as it removed the social pressure to be constantly connected.”

—High school teacher, North Carolina
