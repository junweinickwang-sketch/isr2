Over Half of Public School Leaders State That Cell Phones Detrimentally Affect Academic Performance

A majority of schools maintain policies that limit cell phone usage during instructional time, with many identifying negative consequences for attention and academic results.

WASHINGTON (February 19, 2025) — New data from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) reveals the degree to which public schools have instituted policies to curtail cell phone use. The most recent findings from the School Pulse Panel (SPP) show that 77 percent of public schools forbid students from using cell phones during any class period. A greater proportion of elementary schools (86 percent) have enacted such a policy when compared to high or secondary schools (55 percent). Furthermore, 38 percent of schools that have cell phone policies also enforce restrictions on cell phone use outside of the classroom, for instance, during free periods, between classes, or at extracurricular events. It is notable that 30 percent of schools forbid cell phone use during all classes in addition to these non-instructional situations.

“The latest School Pulse Panel data underscores that school leaders perceive cell phones as more than a simple classroom distraction,” stated NCES Commissioner Peggy Carr. “With 53 percent of school leaders reporting adverse impacts of cell phone use on academic performance, and an even higher number noting negative effects on students’ mental health and attention spans, schools are confronting a significant issue. Educational institutions are responding with practical measures, such as banning or limiting phone use.”

The School Pulse Panel additionally offers insights concerning artificial intelligence (AI) within public schools. A favorable perspective on teachers utilizing AI for their professional responsibilities is held by 69 percent of leaders. Public school administrators moderately or strongly concur that the integration of AI tools into teaching and learning will simplify tasks for teachers (72 percent), allow teachers to be more aligned with current teaching and learning requirements (70 percent), and permit teachers to become more effective educators (67 percent). Training on the application of AI is provided by 67 percent of schools to all or some teachers, staff, and/or administrators, although variations exist depending on school characteristics. Approximately half (47 percent) of schools educate some or all of their students about AI, with this figure being higher for high/secondary schools (72 percent), schools with 1,000 or more students (69 percent), and middle/combined schools (59 percent). Written policies regarding student AI use in school exist at only 31 percent of all public schools, and about one-third of public schools (32 percent) have instructors who employ software to detect whether a student's assignment has been generated by AI.

In the domain of school facilities and infrastructure, a majority of public schools (87 percent) state they possess sufficient space for their student body, with 24 percent depending on portable or modular structures. High-speed internet is accessible to students throughout all school grounds and buildings at more than three-quarters of public schools (77 percent). Some form of cooling system is present in nearly all schools (98 percent), central heating is available throughout the entire main instructional building in most schools (87 percent), and the overall indoor air quality is rated as “good” or “excellent” by 80 percent of schools.

The findings made public today are derived from an experimental data product of the School Pulse Panel, which polls public K-12 schools across the United States about high-priority education topics on a monthly basis during the academic year. The SPP data, gathered from December 6–20, 2024, originated from 1,490 participating public K-12 schools located in every state and the District of Columbia.

Additional data gathered from 99 public K-12 schools in the U.S. Outlying Areas—which include American Samoa, the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands, Guam, and the U.S. Virgin Islands—are also accessible. The results from this data collection reveal that 36 percent of public schools in the Outlying Areas prohibit students from using cell phones during any class.

Experimental data products represent innovative statistical tools developed using new data sources or methodologies. While experimental data might not satisfy all of NCES’s quality benchmarks, they provide sufficient value to data users, especially when other relevant products are unavailable, to warrant their release. Key Findings:

Technology Devices

  • Over three-quarters of public schools (77 percent) maintain a cell phone policy that forbids students from having their mobile phone during any classes. Concurrently, 12 percent have a cell phone policy that permits teachers to determine if students can possess their cell phones during class, and 5 percent have policies that let students have their cell phones throughout all classes. Among public schools, 6 percent indicated they do not have a policy regarding students having cell phones in the classroom.
    • In comparison to the national figure (77 percent), a larger percentage of elementary schools (86 percent) have a cell phone policy prohibiting students from having their cell phones during any classes, whereas a smaller percentage of high/secondary schools (55 percent) and schools with 1,000 or more students (61 percent) have this kind of policy.
  • Thirty-eight percent of public schools with a cell phone policy forbid their students from using their mobile phones in scenarios outside of class time (i.e., during “free periods,” between class periods, at extracurricular activities, during mealtimes, outside of school buildings but still on school property, and in other situations).
  • In total, 30 percent of all public schools have a cell phone policy that disallows students from having their cell phone in any class or from using their cell phone at school.
  • More than half of public school administrators (53 percent) believe that their students' academic achievement has been negatively affected by cell phone usage. Over two-thirds perceive that cell phones have had a detrimental impact on their students' mental health (72 percent) and attention span (73 percent). Overall, 52 percent of public school leaders believe that their students have been negatively affected in these three domains, while 9 percent feel students have been positively affected in these three domains.
  • Approximately 9 in 10 public schools (88 percent) operate a 1-to-1 computing program that furnishes every student with a school-provided device, like a laptop or tablet, for the 2024–25 school year. Among these institutions:
    • Laptops are made available to students by 89 percent of these schools, and tablets are made available by 27 percent.
    • Students are permitted to take their device home on school days and weekends by 46 percent of these schools, whereas 37 percent do not allow students to take their device home.
      • Compared to the national figure (46 percent), a greater percentage of schools with the following attributes permit students to take their device home on school days and weekends:
        • Schools having 1,000 or more students (86 percent)
        • High/secondary schools (80 percent)
        • Middle/combined schools (67 percent)
      • Compared to the national figure (46 percent), a smaller percentage of schools with the following attributes permit students to take their device home on school days and weekends:
        • Schools having 300–499 students (36 percent)
        • Schools located in high poverty neighborhoods (34 percent)
        • Elementary schools (23 percent)

Artificial Intelligence

  • Regarding their students' use of AI for educational purposes, 42 percent of public school leaders indicated a favorable opinion, while an unfavorable opinion was reported by 31 percent.
    • The specified percentage of public school leaders “agreed” with the declaration “Integrating AI tools into teaching and learning will:
      • allow students at their school to cultivate superior critical thinking skills (39 percent); and
      • result in improved educational outcomes for students at their school (38 percent).”
  • Concerning their teachers' use of AI for professional duties, a favorable view was reported by 69 percent of public school leaders, whereas an unfavorable view was reported by 13 percent.
    • The specified percentage of public school leaders agreed with the declaration “Integrating AI tools into teaching and learning will:
      • make work easier for teachers at their school (72 percent);
      • allow teachers at their school to be more in step with current teaching and learning needs (70 percent); and
      • allow teachers at their school to be more proficient educators (67 percent).”
  • In relation to policies on students’ application of AI in school, a written policy at their school or district was reported by 31 percent of public school leaders, while 18 percent reported that their school or district lacks a policy at present but they know of plans to create one. A policy is not in place and they are unaware of any current plans to create one, as reported by 30 percent. One out of five public school leaders (20 percent) was unsure if their school or district has a written policy on students’ use of AI in school.
  • Training on the use of AI was provided by about two-thirds of public schools (67 percent) to all or some teachers, staff, and/or administrators this school year, and training was provided to all teachers by 26 percent.
    • In comparison to the national figure (67 percent), a larger percentage of schools with fewer than 25 percent students of color (75 percent) offered training on AI use to teachers, staff, and/or administrators this school year, while a smaller percentage of schools with more than 75 percent students of color did so (58 percent).
  • Approximately 7 in 10 public schools (73 percent) indicated that they have at least a few teachers who utilize AI for one or more of these tasks: developing lesson plans, handling administrative duties, designing customized educational materials for students, augmenting instruction, building diagnostic assessments, building formative assessments, or for grading and feedback.
  • Instruction on AI is provided to students by around half of public schools (47 percent).
    • Compared to the national figure (47 percent), a greater percentage of schools with the following attributes indicated that they provide instruction to students about AI:
      • High/secondary schools (72 percent)
      • Schools with 1,000 or more students (69 percent)
      • Middle/combined schools (59 percent)
    • Compared to the national figure (47 percent), a smaller percentage of schools with the following attributes indicated that they provide instruction to students about AI:
      • Schools with 300–499 students (40 percent)
      • Schools where the student body comprises more than 75 percent students of color (38 percent)
      • Elementary schools (32 percent)
  • About one-third of public school leaders (32 percent) stated that they have one or more teachers at their school who employ software to determine if a student’s assignment has been generated by AI.

Digital Literacy

  • Training in digital literacy is provided to students by 68 percent of public schools. Among these institutions, 61 percent indicated that this training is delivered as part of a formal or structured digital literacy curriculum.
    • The two most frequently cited methods for students to receive digital literacy education are in a library or media lab (57 percent) and as an integrated part of core subjects like English language arts, social studies, math, and/or science (54 percent).

School Facilities

  • A majority of public schools (87 percent) state that their school building(s) is(are) sufficient for the size of their student population, with 24 percent reporting this is the case through the use of portable classrooms or modular school buildings, and 63 percent reporting this is the case without using portable classrooms or modular school buildings.
  • High-speed internet is available to students across all school grounds and buildings, as reported by more than three-quarters of public schools (77 percent). Furthermore, 21 percent indicated that high-speed internet is accessible to students within all school buildings but not across all school grounds, and 1 percent have high-speed internet in only select rooms of their school buildings.
  • A cooling system of some kind, such as central air conditioning (74 percent) and individual thermostat-controlled cooling units in specific rooms (42 percent), is present in nearly all public schools (98 percent).
  • Central heating throughout the entire primary instructional building is available in most public schools (87 percent).
  • The indoor air quality at their school was reported as “good” or “excellent” by eighty percent of public schools.
  • When questioned about the condition of various building components of their main instructional building, 25 percent of public school leaders indicated that all components were in “good” or “excellent” condition, while 18 percent reported that at least one of the components requires replacement.

Daily Attendance

  • As reported by school leaders, the average daily student attendance at public schools during this month's data collection period was 92 percent.

Technical Note

Statistics derived from sample surveys may be influenced by sampling and non-sampling errors. All comparisons presented in this statistical press release have undergone testing and have been determined to be statistically significant, unless specified otherwise. Statistical tests conducted by NCES are typically performed at a 95 percent confidence level. Further information concerning the methodology, which includes the survey questionnaire, can be located on the School Pulse Panel methodology web page.

Definitions

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is defined as a machine-based system capable of making predictions, recommendations, or decisions in an automated fashion through analysis.

Digital literacy pertains to the ability to utilize technology for finding, assessing, organizing, creating, and sharing information.

The following definitions were provided for rating the features of the main instructional building, which include roofs, floors, foundation, exterior (walls, ceilings, or doors), interior (walls, ceilings, or doors), plumbing, heating, ventilation, and/or air conditioning systems (HVAC), electrical power, indoor lighting, and exterior lighting:

  • Excellent: The feature is new or can be easily restored to a “like new” state; only minimal routine upkeep is necessary.
  • Good: Some preventative maintenance and/or corrective repairs are needed.
  • Fair: The feature fails to meet functional requirements in some instances; significant corrective maintenance and repair are necessary.
  • Poor: The feature exhibits consistent substandard performance; failures are disruptive and expensive; it demands constant attention, major corrective repair, or a complete overhaul.
  • Needs Replacement: The feature is non-operational and requires replacement.
