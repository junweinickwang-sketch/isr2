Scroll Less, Learn More: State-Level Policy Strategies to Address the Influence of Smartphones and Social Media in Education

Blog
December 31, 2024

Explore how state-level policymakers are able to minimize classroom disturbances from smartphones and social media, thereby improving learning results and guaranteeing student achievement. Gain insights into the harmful consequences of prolonged screen exposure and examine pragmatic, student-oriented policy measures designed to foster focused and interactive educational settings.

The proliferation of smartphones and social media is connected with negative consequences for youth. Scientific studies, encompassing discoveries by the American Academy of Pediatrics and observations from social psychologist Jonathan Haidt, link extensive screen time to higher incidences of anxiety, depression, and insufficient sleep in children and adolescents. It has been noted that young girls are especially susceptible to the detrimental effects of social media.

Furthermore, it is understood that educational results are jeopardized, which represents our primary concern as we endeavor to reshape K-12 education to guarantee the success of each child through student-focused, state-level policy initiatives. What measures can be taken to reduce distractions within the classroom, enabling students to learn effectively and allowing educators to perform their duties?

The Impact of Phones in Educational Settings: Insights from Data Analysis

Recent data published toward the end of last year by the International Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) clearly illustrates the relationship between the amount of time spent on screens and academic achievement. 

According to the OECD findings, the most effective method for removing classroom distractions involves a complete prohibition of cell phones on school grounds. The information additionally indicates that a greater amount of leisure time dedicated by students to devices and social media, particularly during school hours, corresponds with poorer academic results in mathematics.

Causal research also substantiates this assertion. The availability of cell phones within classrooms interferes with the educational experience, which is demonstrated by falling test results and a rise in reported classroom disturbances, with students from low-income backgrounds frequently being the most impacted.

A vast majority of educators voice apprehension regarding the detrimental effects of cell phones on the concentration of students, as well as on their social-emotional growth and mental well-being.

State Actions on the Cell Phone Issue and ExcelinEd's Proposals for State Intervention

A number of states have already enacted legislative measures to address these problems. In the year 2024, a minimum of 19 states approved legislation to limit cell phone usage in schools, and no fewer than 10 states enacted bills designed to regulate social media access for minors. An overview of significant state legislation is provided at the conclusion of this article.

The recommendations that follow detail strategies that both state education departments and policymakers can utilize to reinforce cell phone and social media regulations in their states, along with examples of pioneering states that have already taken such steps.

Implementing a Statewide Ban on Cell Phones During School Hours

States ought to put in place a thorough ban on the use of cell phones throughout the school day. Allowances can be made for educational activities when supervised by a teacher or in case of emergencies.

Although it is not a policy for the full school day, Florida's law stands out as the first to forbid cell phone use during teaching periods while also integrating wider digital wellness initiatives. Moreover, Orange County Schools in Florida advanced this by prohibiting cell phone use for the entire duration of the school day, which resulted in greater student engagement.

In both Virginia and Arkansas, new programs are being introduced to curtail the use of cell phones within schools. Virginia's Governor Youngkin has released an executive order that tasks the department of education with creating guidelines for the implementation of policies that establish a cell phone-free setting. Furthermore, Virginia plans to provide microgrants to assist schools in applying best practices for controlling cell phone usage, and Arkansas will finance a pilot initiative that supplies pouches for students to secure their phones during school hours.

The Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD), which is the second-largest school district in the country, has recently sanctioned a policy that forbids the use of cell phones throughout the school day. Public schools in New York City also declared that they will be instituting a comparable ban, although the specific details have not been made public yet.

Providing Districts with Implementation Support: Model Policies, Financial Aid, and Mental Health Resources

States are advised to offer model policies for school districts to implement concerning the use of cell phones in schools. As an illustration, Ohio's model policy forbids student cell phone usage at all times (with exceptions for students who have health issues or individualized education plans) and specifies the disciplinary actions for any infractions.

States should allocate funds for schools to obtain phone storage options, like lockers or pouches, to keep devices secure during school hours. The implementation of Yondr pouches in the Bethlehem Central School District brought about a “completely transformational” change in student results, participation, and school culture. A recent study also revealed that following the adoption of Yondr, student academic success rates rose by as much as 6.27%, which was complemented by a 44% reduction in the average monthly number of behavioral referrals.

Lastly, states are encouraged to allocate mental health funding to back the initiatives of school districts. In the previous year, Pennsylvania designated $100 million in additional funds to schools for student mental health services. Lawmakers are currently suggesting a pilot initiative for schools to utilize lockable phone bags, financed by the state, pointing to a link between the increase in smartphone use and a decline in mental health.

Passing Legislation to Limit Social Media Access for Young People

States ought to contemplate passing laws that increase the minimum age required for creating social media accounts and necessitate parental approval. They should also guarantee that these platforms adhere to age verification and data security regulations, and limit social media companies from employing addictive algorithms.

Florida and various other states have passed legislation to confirm the age of individuals using social media and to mandate parental approval. Tennessee obligates social media firms to confirm the age of their users and to secure parental consent for minors within a 14-day period after an account is created. The state also insists that companies furnish parents with instruments to oversee their children's accounts, which includes privacy options, daily time limits, and mandatory breaks. In 2023, 13 states enacted 23 laws concerning the safety of children online.

New York's “Stop Addictive Feeds Exploitation (SAFE) for Kids” legislation prohibits social media companies from using addictive algorithms on the social media accounts of minors. The content is required to be presented in chronological order. Furthermore, New York's Child Data Protection Act limits the gathering and distribution of data from users who are under the age of 18.

Supplying Parents with Essential Information and Resources

Parents have a critical function in helping to make sure that students restrict their access to cell phones and social media. States can offer materials for districts to distribute, which detail standard methods and messages for parents, such as:

  • Postponing the introduction of smartphones by supporting campaigns like the “Wait until 8th” initiative. By motivating parents to collectively agree to delay providing smartphones to their children, these efforts can help diminish peer pressure and advocate for sensible alternatives such as basic phones for calls only and watches.
  • Tools that parents can subscribe to for enhanced awareness and supervision of their student's cell phone and internet usage.
  • Materials on promoting more independent play and engagement in outdoor activities.

Enact Sensible Laws for Childhood Independence

These statutes, made popular by the “Let Grow” movement, empower parents to give their children chances to develop resilience without the apprehension of criminal charges or investigations by social services. Such laws have been approved with significant bipartisan backing in Utah, Oklahoma, Texas, Colorado, Virginia, Illinois, and Connecticut.

Creating and Applying Standards for Digital Literacy

Digital literacy is essential because it reinforces more extensive digital citizenship education, especially in classroom environments where technologies such as laptops are common. This involves instructing students on how to interact with digital platforms responsibly, utilize technology for research and communication, assess news and information sources with a critical eye, and comprehend the effects of social media on mental health and overall well-being.

New Jersey requires K-12 information literacy education as part of the New Jersey Student Learning Standards, which includes digital, visual, media, textual, and technological literacy.

Final Summary

State policymakers have a vital part in lessening cell phone-related distractions in the classroom to cultivate a more favorable learning atmosphere. By passing laws that establish definite regulations on cell phone use, aiding schools in creating technology-free areas, and advancing digital literacy initiatives, policymakers can markedly diminish the adverse effects of mobile devices on the academic achievements of students.

By employing a mix of regulation, education, and assistance, state policymakers can contribute to the creation of classrooms where educators can instruct and students can concentrate, participate, and prosper without the continuous interruption from cell phones.
