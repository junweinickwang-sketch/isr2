The Pros and Cons of School Cell Phone Bans: An Analysis of Safety, Benefits, and Disadvantages

Written By: J. Lasswell

October 1, 2024

Does prohibiting cell phones in educational institutions resolve safety issues? This article delves into the advantages and disadvantages of such restrictions, evaluating their impact on student well-being, learning outcomes, and mental health.

Contemporary issues often demand contemporary solutions; however, these modern solutions frequently introduce new challenges. The use of cell phones by students in schools exemplifies this situation, sparking a nationwide discussion among families, school administrators, and legislators who are now contemplating action. Major school systems, such as the Los Angeles school district, along with entire states like New York and California, are evaluating statewide limitations on mobile device usage during the school day, making it crucial to examine all perspectives through the lens of safety. By analyzing adolescent psychology research, data on cell phone usage, and firsthand accounts from emergency responders, it is possible to determine whether cell phones genuinely enhance student safety or merely create a false sense of security. This analysis also seeks to identify a balanced approach that satisfies the needs of educators, administrators, students, and parents alike.

Typically, perspectives on having cell phones in the classroom vary based on one's role, as students and parents often desire uninterrupted access to their devices, whereas teachers and administrators feel overwhelmed by the persistent disruptions they cause.

A report published by the U.S. Surgeon General indicated that American adolescents between the ages of 12 and 15 who dedicated over three hours daily to social media were at twice the risk of developing poor mental health outcomes, such as symptoms of depression and anxiety. In the post-COVID-19 era in America, between 16.3% and 66.4% of children are reported to spend four or more hours each day on their smartphones. A ban on cell phones in the classroom could serve as more than just a method to eliminate constant distractions; it might also mitigate the cumulative negative mental health impacts of social media consumption, which stands as the second leading reason for smartphone use among teenagers.

Educators are experiencing increasing challenges in redirecting their students' focus from their phones back to the educational material. Research conducted by Common Sense Media revealed that teenagers get an average of 273 notifications each day. It is noteworthy that almost one-fourth of these alerts are received during school hours. It is therefore not surprising that teachers' unions throughout the nation are spearheading the movement to prohibit cell phones within classrooms. According to the Pew Research Center, an overwhelming majority of teachers (72%) identify cell phone distractions as a major problem within their teaching environments.

“Anytime a student was instructed to put away their phone, it would become a point of contention. The ability for students to communicate with peers outside of our school during school hours would lead to our students, particularly those in high school, acting out,” stated Becky Carr, an educator at a school in Pennsylvania. “Boys would attempt to impress, and girls would focus solely on capturing the attention of their male peers—even the capacity to listen to specific genres of music can amplify our students' behavioral issues.”

With Pew research indicating that 95% of teenagers now possess a smartphone, the distractions students face extend beyond simple text messaging. Teachers across the country have observed students engaging in online shopping, listening to music, browsing social media, watching live videos, and participating in numerous other activities that divert their focus from their studies. These interruptions are demonstrably affecting students' academic results. The decline in U.S. math and reading scores, along with surveys indicating a troubling increase in student behavioral issues, illustrates a clear problem: educators are urgently seeking to restore order in their classrooms, with cell phones being a central element of the issue. Michael Rich, an associate professor of pediatrics at Harvard Medical School, explained to The Harvard Gazette that phones and school are simply incompatible; students cannot effectively process information while facing constant interruptions. “The human brain is unable to concentrate on more than one thing at a time,” he noted. “Consequently, what we perceive as multitasking is in reality rapid task-switching. The issue with this is that while task-switching might cover various topics, it fails to delve deeply into any single one.”

For a large number of educators and policymakers, the solution appears straightforward: forbid cell phone use during instructional time. States such as Indiana and Ohio have enacted laws that limit cell phone use in classrooms, while Oklahoma, Florida, New York, California, Washington, Kansas, Vermont, and Connecticut are currently debating comparable measures. In addition to state-level actions, an even greater number of school districts are independently establishing their own restrictions on cell phone use within their schools.

This wave of legislative action highlights a significant challenge that educators are encountering nationwide, but the question remains whether these policies truly enhance safety. Many students report feeling more secure when they have a direct means of communication with their parents during the school day. Evan, a middle school student from northwest Indiana, commented, “I certainly feel safer in class having my phone with me because if I need my dad or my mom, I can reach them more quickly and keep them updated on what is happening.”

Although feeling secure at school is a crucial component of a student's well-being, genuine safety extends beyond a mere feeling or perception. It is argued by some that students were able to remain safe in school long before the advent of cellphone technology, and that these devices offer little assistance during a real emergency. Dr. Beth Sanborn, a seasoned school resource officer, recalled past discussions with parents, stating, “When I raised concerns with parents about their child’s cell phone use, the common response was ‘my child needs to be able to call me if there’s an emergency.’ However, none could elaborate on what actions they would take in such a situation.” Beyond their use for reporting an emergency or reconnecting with parents, cell phones in the possession of students provide minimal help in a crisis. Nevertheless, we are in an era of unprecedented challenges, marked by more frequent and deadlier school shootings. The perpetrators of these attacks are employing more advanced firearms, utilizing technology to convey threats, and using digital tools to gather information on their targets—so, should students not be granted the ability to use the same technology during an active shooter event?

During the 2013 active shooter incident at Arapahoe High School in Colorado, students utilized their cell phones to get in touch with their parents while they were sheltering in place. Some students even shared their devices with peers so they could also contact their families, while others used their phones to confirm the safety of siblings who were located in different parts of the school. Although possessing these devices did not alter their immediate physical safety, it undeniably had a positive effect on the emotional well-being of the school community throughout the crisis.

“Drawing from my background in emergency response, I consistently observe that first responders and those caught in a critical incident are desperate for information,” remarks critical response specialist James Dunleavy. “At first glance, I would contend that if students have the means to convey life-saving information with their cell phones, they should be allowed to have them.” Dunleavy does, however, acknowledge that a certain level of nuance is necessary when considering student cell phone access at school: “Yet, we are all aware that the daily chaos in our nation's schools is unrelated to emergency response… Like many one-size-fits-all solutions, there is too much variation in the cell phone issue across different districts, counties, and states. Cell phone usage in schools should be governed by a policy managed at the district or school level, tailored to their specific circumstances.”

Statewide prohibitions might not address the unique requirements of various school communities, yet many schools have discovered solutions that are yielding encouraging outcomes. A middle school in Connecticut has achieved success by having students secure their cell phones in locked pouches, which are then put away during class time. These pouches, developed by Yondr, permit students to access their devices in designated areas by touching the pouch to a magnetic unlocking base. Implementing solutions that restrict cell phone use during class but permit it between classes, during free periods, and at other suitable times could represent the compromise that schools are seeking.

Although any adjustments to cell phone policies are likely to face resistance from students, a significant number of them have expressed positive feelings about the introduction of cell phone pouches like those from Yondr or similar products. Regarding the recent adoption of Yondr pouches at her school, Junior Allie Hixon remarked to The Akron Journal, “I didn't have to get involved with all the drama…I feel like I am more focused in my classes.”

The learning environment in classrooms has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past decade. With the widespread adoption of smartphones and the escalation of behavioral and academic challenges exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic, the teaching profession has become more demanding than ever. Educational institutions are facing severe understaffing as disheartened teachers exit the field in large numbers. Data from Pew Research as of 2023 indicates that approximately 30% of teachers intend to seek new employment within the next year, and 40% of that group are looking to leave the education sector entirely. While issues like compensation and resource availability are significant contributors to this trend, it is crucial to concentrate on solutions that empower teachers to instruct effectively; otherwise, academic achievement and student behavioral health will likely continue to decline. Solutions regarding cell phone use at school must also take into account the significant fear of violence that nearly all U.S. students experience, and maintaining a line of communication with their parents or guardians can greatly enhance student well-being. Therefore, strategies that permit students to use their phones at appropriate times outside of class should be evaluated in every educational setting.
