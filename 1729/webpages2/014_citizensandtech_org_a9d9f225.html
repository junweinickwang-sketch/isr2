Exploring Community Strategies for Implementing and Managing School Cell Phone Prohibitions

Prohibitions on mobile phones in educational settings are often presented as a critical solution for student distraction in classrooms. Although these bans may appear to be a straightforward measure, the practical application involves navigating intricate details by local schools and community organizations. This raises the question of what approaches communities are taking to enforce school cell phone restrictions when mandated, and what insights can be gained from these local initiatives?

In the upcoming autumn, New York will align with numerous other states by instituting a ban on student use of internet-capable mobile devices in K-12 schools during school hours, a policy shift that will affect learners across all grade levels. More than 700 educational bodies, including school districts, charter schools, and cooperative educational services, must now implement a comprehensive "bell-to-bell" prohibition on school property, while also aiming to mitigate the discrimination and excessive policing observed in other jurisdictions with comparable restrictions. This new policy receives considerable backing from both parent organizations and teachers' unions, with 72% of American high school educators identifying cellphone distraction as a significant issue within the classroom. Data from 2024 indicates that 68% of the American public favored some form of phone restrictions in schools; however, only one-third of respondents endorsed the type of complete, all-day bans now being implemented in New York.

Given the extensive reach of this policy, researchers have a significant opportunity, and arguably a responsibility, to assist the public and policymakers in understanding its real-world impacts. This legislation represents one of the most impactful and synchronized technology policy interventions of our era, effectively severing a primary communication channel for a substantial number of New York's 2.4 million students. Furthermore, because previous studies have not produced conclusive evidence regarding the advantages or disadvantages of phone bans, this implementation serves as a collective learning experience for everyone involved.

A dependable assessment of the cell phone ban's effects cannot be achieved merely by calculating statewide before-and-after statistical differences. The reason for this complexity is that every school and community is unique, leading to significant variations in the specific policies and enforcement methods adopted by each school district. Local communities are tasked with determining who will be responsible for policy enforcement, the associated costs, procedures for handling exceptions, and strategies to prevent the policy from exacerbating existing inequalities. Additionally, administrators and educators across the educational system are preparing to address significant resistance from the student body. Therefore, any trustworthy evaluation of New York's ban must meticulously consider these specific, community-level implementation details.

At the Citizens and Technology Lab, our focus on public research collaborations means we are particularly well-suited to examine these granular, community-level factors. For this reason, we are mobilizing hundreds of Cornell students this year to gather firsthand information on how local communities are debating, formulating, and modifying their cell phone ban policies as circumstances evolve.

Every autumn, I instruct an undergraduate course titled Communication and Technology, in which students are taught to analyze the design, psychological, and societal dimensions of technologies ranging from mobile phones to artificial intelligence. Correspondingly, each fall semester, I design a research project that leverages the combined efforts of several hundred students who have connections across New York state. As an example, two years prior, students in the course examined the rollout of New York City's algorithm transparency law, a project which was featured in the Wall Street Journal. The previous year, our project involved a randomized controlled trial to compare the effectiveness of generative AI for writing feedback against feedback provided by student peers. This academic year, our focus is on investigating the New York State cell phone ban.

Fundamentally, the students will assemble and scrutinize a research repository of public dialogues concerning the ban for each school district statewide, encompassing school board meeting records, local news coverage, the original policies submitted to the state, and ideally, any policy revisions that emerge throughout the year due to on-the-ground developments. In addition, we aim to conduct more detailed interviews with a diverse array of crucial local stakeholders from a chosen subset of schools. Our goal is that by the semester's conclusion, we will have an initial understanding of the strategies school districts chose to implement and the lessons they garnered during the initial months of enforcing these campus-wide policies.

We invite school administrators, community leaders, school board members, student leaders, and engaged parents who wish to contribute to this research to please contact Andrew Restieri at ar2349@cornell.edu and J. Nathan Matias at nathan.matias@cornell.edu.
