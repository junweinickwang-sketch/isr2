Developing School Cell Phone Policies: A Mental Health Guide for Educational Leaders

Throughout the United States, initiatives at the local, state, and federal levels are emerging to prohibit or substantially restrict cell phone and similar device usage within K-12 classrooms. While the adoption of phone-free policies can serve as an effective strategy for enhancing student learning, behavior, and general well-being, The Jed Foundation (JED) acknowledges the complexity of phone use on school grounds and the distinct requirements of each school and community. When you are adopting a cell phone policy for your educational institution or district, successful execution requires more than simply distributing policy details to parents, caregivers, students, and faculty.

JED's Recommended Guidelines for a Balanced Approach

JED highly advises adopting a balanced and strategic methodology, which involves engaging all stakeholders in cooperative, significant, and constructive discussions that focus on the particular needs of every school and district. Gaining the support of the community is crucial for guaranteeing a seamless implementation, providing effective support for student well-being, and continuously monitoring and improving the policy. JED suggests that schools take the following actions:

Assemble a diverse team of stakeholders and gather their input through surveys. This team should be composed of administrators, educators, caregivers, and students. Every group offers a distinct viewpoint and specialized knowledge, which are all vital for crafting an effective policy, guaranteeing its successful execution, and facilitating continuous support and feedback.

Understand the necessary special considerations. It is important to recognize that exceptions and specific considerations exist for particular student populations regarding cell phone usage. 

  • Within public schools, whether district or charter, cell phones can be permitted to address medical or accessibility requirements as outlined in IEPs and 504 plans, which furnish essential accommodations. Similar to any other accommodation, this necessity must be officially recorded in the student's plan. Nevertheless, students remain accountable for adhering to the parts of a cell phone policy that do not pertain to these specific accommodations.
  • Residential or boarding schools are required to establish supplementary policies that address the availability of traditional phone lines or create structured time slots after school hours specifically for cell phone usage.

Establish and enforce policies that are both clear and consistent. Work in collaboration with your stakeholder group to formulate unambiguous, unified policies and offer professional development to ensure they are understood and can be upheld by all staff members. Prior to announcing a ban or restriction to the broader school community, it is crucial to discuss with teachers how your school plans to manage phone storage in a manner that is simple to implement across the entire school.

Develop a comprehensive and clear communication strategy. While communicating with stakeholders, it is important to stress the underlying reasons for the policy. These reasons might encompass:

  • The growing trend of students using mobile devices for non-educational activities during class time.
  • The significant distractions originating from social media platforms and various forms of entertainment.
  • The escalating problem of cyberbullying among students.
  • The creation of challenges that impede the learning process.
  • The potential for academic dishonesty on tests and assignments.
  • Concerns regarding the privacy rights of both staff and students.
  • The negative impact on mental health outcomes, such as increased anxiety, depression, and feelings of social isolation.

Distribute evidence-based practices or case studies from educational institutions or districts that have effectively put cell phone policies into practice. Presenting this evidence helps to illustrate the viability of the proposed policy, foresee possible challenges, and demonstrate how proven strategies can be customized for your school's or district's specific circumstances. This approach can build greater confidence in the policy, which contributes to its ultimate success.

Establish a practical timeline for the policy's implementation. Allow sufficient time for all stakeholders to prepare for and respond to the new policy. Addressing any questions that emerge during this period will enable you to make necessary adjustments to your plans.

Incorporate digital and social media literacy education directly into the school curriculum. Instruct students on the principles of ethical, responsible, and constructive technology usage throughout all academic subjects.

Organize open forums for discussion. Allocate specific times to listen to questions and address concerns, which could take place during a student assembly or a parent-teacher organization meeting for educators and families.

Offer training and engagement opportunities for the community and caregivers. Dedicate time for consistent training sessions for both educators and caregivers, focusing on technological advancements and promoting balanced technology use.

Launch informational campaigns across the entire school. The goal is to educate the school community about technology use in a way that eliminates stigma and promotes well-informed decision-making.

Employ classroom strategies that are both innovative and practical. Equip staff with the necessary tools and motivational incentives required for the effective implementation of phone policies.

What Are the Reasons for Implementing Limited Cell Phone Use Policies?

A growing concern exists among both educators and caregivers regarding students' escalating use of phones for non-educational purposes during class and its subsequent effect on academic achievement. Statistical data supports these concerns, and this information can be valuable in discussions with stakeholders.

  • According to a poll from the National Education Association, 83% of surveyed teachers are in favor of policies that keep schools phone-free for the entire day.
  • A 2023 comprehensive report by Common Sense Media on teenage smartphone use revealed that over half of children with a smartphone get an average of 237 daily notifications, with 23% of these alerts arriving during school hours.
  • Research from a McAfee survey indicated that 21% of high school students confess to utilizing their phones for cheating in an academic setting.
  • A study from the Norwegian Institute of Public Health demonstrated that after a three-year, full-time smartphone ban was enacted in schools, middle-school girls reported a 46% decrease in bullying incidents, and boys reported a 43% decrease.
  • The American Psychological Association has reported that 41% of teenagers who use social media most frequently describe their overall mental health as poor or very poor, in contrast to just 23% of teens who use social media the least.

What Are the Different Approaches to Reducing Phone Use in Schools?

Understanding the various types of cell phone use policies is crucial, even if you have a drafted policy prepared for implementation. This knowledge will assist you in offering the right support and engaging in knowledgeable discussions about how your district's policy measures up against others. The primary categories of policies include:

Complete Cell Phone Prohibitions. This policy requires students' phones to be secured and inaccessible for the whole school day, stored in one of the following locations:

  • Student lockers
  • Specialized storage pouches that are unlocked at day's end, similar to retail security devices.
  • Secure containers kept in the main administrative office.
  • Storage pouches located in the homeroom or the final classroom of the day.

Partial Prohibitions. This approach permits students to use their phones during specific times, such as lunchtime, study periods, or another designated schoolwide window for device access. Enforcing a partial ban can present more challenges, as it necessitates a phone storage solution that is both secure and readily accessible to students throughout the day.

Teacher Discretion for Classroom Phone Use. This policy functions more as a limited-use guideline than a complete ban on cell phones. It grants individual teachers the authority to determine when and how students can use phones within their classrooms. For example, a math instructor might permit the use of a phone's calculator function. Such policies can create stress for both educators and learners. Teachers are required to define and consistently uphold a clear system for phone storage and use. Meanwhile, students might struggle to remember the different rules for each class, leading to anxiety about fulfilling varied expectations.

Education Week provides a tracker that monitors which states have implemented bans or restrictions on cell phones in schools. 

What Are the Advantages and Disadvantages of School Cell Phone Bans?

Similar to any discussion concerning teenagers and their use of online platforms, providing access to cell phones during school hours presents both advantages and drawbacks. Recognizing the potential benefits can facilitate more detailed and balanced conversations with parents, caregivers, students, staff, and other community members. The following points outline some of the key benefits for each perspective: 

What Are the Benefits of Allowing Cell Phone Use in Schools?

  • It enables the use of educational and polling applications to enhance student engagement.
  • It offers access to applications for stress reduction and other resources that support mental wellness.
  • It provides students and teachers with immediate access to crisis management resources, such as the 988 hotline, the Crisis Text Line, and the Say Something program from Sandy Hook Promise.
  • It permits the utilization of collaborative tools for completing group projects. 

What Are the Benefits of Banning Cell Phone Use in Schools?

  • It enhances student focus and attention while minimizing classroom distractions.
  • It creates more opportunities for direct, face-to-face engagement and social interaction.
  • It relieves teachers of the duty to monitor phone usage and enforce policies, freeing up more time for instruction and ensuring more consistent enforcement across classrooms.
  • It reduces the probability of cyberbullying occurring during the school day.

How to Find Support for Implementing Cell Phone Policies

Any newly implemented phone restrictions are likely to elicit a broad spectrum of responses from students, teachers, parents, and caregivers. While many will probably embrace the change, some individuals, particularly students, might experience feelings of frustration, anxiety, anger, or other adverse emotions. 

For those participating in a JED school program, your designated School or District Mental Health Specialist is available to assist in drafting policies, developing communication plans, and connecting you with other schools that have already put restrictions in place. If your school is not currently enrolled in one of our programs, we encourage you to contact us to discuss the various ways JED can provide support.
