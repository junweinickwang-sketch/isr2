Nationwide Calls to Ban Phones in K-12 Schools: Key Considerations for Private Schools on Whether to Adopt a Phone-Free Policy

Published: July 1, 2024

Across the United States, state governments and public school systems are advocating for bans on cellphones in educational settings, citing significant classroom disruptions and adverse impacts on students as primary reasons. With an increasing number of schools evaluating phone-free policies and lawmakers urging for statewide measures, private educational institutions are similarly experiencing mounting pressure from various stakeholders. This analysis will examine the advantages and disadvantages of removing smartphones and comparable devices from school environments, providing essential factors for your institution to consider when formulating such a policy.

Phone-Free Schools: A Growing Trend

In a notable move this June, the Los Angeles public school board passed a resolution aimed at establishing “truly phone-free school days,” highlighting the detrimental effects of excessive cellphone usage on students' mental well-being and academic results. It is anticipated that the district will develop a thorough policy for implementation, which is scheduled to become effective in January 2025. Although L.A. Unified stands as one of the nation's largest districts to proceed with this type of prohibition, it is by no means an isolated case.

The Village School of Naples, a private institution in Florida, announced positive student results in May, two years following the introduction of a policy that forbids mobile phone use throughout the school day (along with a rule that mandates every student's participation in a sports or arts program). A school official reported that “students’ mental wellbeing has improved by 94 percent as measured in the amount of mental health interventions required, and academic achievement has increased significantly as measured in standardized test scores.”

In addition to school-level policies, state legislators and governors, such as those in California and New York, are advocating for laws that would either ban or limit smartphone use in public and charter schools. Furthermore, certain states, including Florida and Indiana, have already enacted laws that regulate wireless communication devices within public schools. The Florida statute also mandates that public schools must “prohibit and prevent students from accessing social media platforms through the use of Internet access provided by the school district,” unless it is for educational activities under a teacher's guidance. (Additionally, a new Florida law extends its reach beyond the school environment. This law, slated to go into effect on January 1, bars children younger than 14 from having social media accounts and mandates parental permission for 14- and 15-year-olds.)

Pros and Cons of Banning or Restricting Phones in School

Advocates for phone-free school environments assert that such bans lead to fewer classroom distractions, a decrease in cyberbullying, enhanced student engagement, and the fostering of healthy social skills. Numerous campaigns also highlight the youth mental health crisis associated with social media consumption and place a strong emphasis on prohibiting smartphones that provide access to these platforms. This specific issue was brought back into focus after the U.S. Surgeon General advocated for warning labels on social media platforms because of their damaging impact on adolescents.

Conversely, those who oppose no-phone regulations in schools argue that the prohibitions are challenging to enforce, eliminate chances to integrate modern technologies into educational activities, and deny students valuable learning experiences. A significant portion of the opposition has originated from parents who express concern about their inability to contact their children during an emergency situation or a crisis scenario such as a school shooting.

Your school must thoughtfully evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of instituting any form of phone policy and should incorporate the viewpoints of students, their families, and the broader school community at an early stage of the decision-making process.

Key Considerations for Your School Policy

Should you determine that a “no-phone” policy is appropriate for your school, the following are several important factors to take into account while creating and executing your policy:

  • Type of Devices. Your policy must clearly define which types of devices will be banned or restricted during school hours. For instance, does your policy cover all cellphones, or is it limited to smartphones with internet access? Does it also apply to other electronics like tablets or smartwatches?
  • Ban vs. Restrictions. A crucial decision is whether to implement a complete ban on these devices or to allow students to possess them with specific restrictions on usage times and methods. While many schools currently limit student cellphone use to unstructured periods like lunch, breaks, and free time, these policies can be harder to enforce than an outright ban.
  • Secured Phone Bags. Certain schools have chosen to mandate that students place their devices into secured, locked pouches upon arriving at campus, which they can then collect before leaving for home. Other schools provide this as an option within individual classrooms at the teacher's discretion, where students must secure their devices before the lesson begins and retrieve them afterward. This approach can fulfill the objective of phone-free school days or classes while still permitting students to have their devices for their commutes, potentially offering parents reassurance and resolving logistical challenges. If you adopt this strategy, your policy needs to detail the specific procedures for device storage and retrieval.
  • Age Groups. You need to decide if the policy will be applicable to every student or just to specific age ranges, like elementary or middle school students. Will the policy be applied consistently across all grades, or will it differ for various school divisions?
  • Enforcement. To ensure your policy is effective, it must detail how it will be enforced. For instance, will you assign specific staff to conduct occasional checks for prohibited devices, or will teachers be responsible for confiscating devices they observe being used against the policy?
  • Consequences for Violations. Your policy must clearly state the spectrum of consequences students may incur for breaching the rules. These consequences could differ based on age and may involve a tiered approach for initial versus subsequent infractions.
  • Exceptions. It may be necessary to provide exceptions or reasonable accommodations for students who have particular medical needs. As an example, students with diabetes frequently use their phones to monitor their insulin levels.
