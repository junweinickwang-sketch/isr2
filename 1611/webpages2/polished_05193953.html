Get smartphones out of K-12 classrooms

Smartphones and other mobile devices are incompatible with the purpose of education  

Heath Madom

Last fall, my grade-level colleagues and I faced a problem: Smartphone usage in our classrooms was rampant, and we struggled to rein it in.

The problem was not that students were scrolling through social media feeds or openly playing Clash Royale. Instead, it was a more under-the-radar sort of usage: checking their phones in repeated bursts — to fire off a quick text or read a notification or, in some cases, watch a quick TikTok. But it was happening many, many times within a single class period, and the resulting distraction was decimating students’ ability to focus on academic tasks.

Trying to solve the problem, we collectively enacted a zero-tolerance smartphone policy at the start of spring semester. We warned students at the start of every class, and after that, we confiscated any phone we saw during the period. We kept track of student infractions across our classes and referred any student who had their phone confiscated more than once to the administration for follow-up. Within days of rolling out the new policy, phone use in our classes effectively evaporated. It felt miraculous.

I share this story, however, not as a scale-up solution, but to illustrate just how problematic phones are in schools today. They’re so problematic that I believe it is time to ban them from school altogether. A number of high schools have done exactly that — and who can blame them? Smartphones stand in direct conflict with the mission of educational spaces. You cannot pay attention to anything else when you are looking at your phone.

One might reasonably object: Why not just enforce no usage policies as stringently as my colleagues and I did? That’s not a solution for two reasons.

First, policing phone use leads to power struggles that harm teacher-student relationships. Like the rest of us, students are intensely attached to their phones; even if you successfully confiscate their phones, you risk damaging your relationship with them. In my own time as a teacher, almost all the serious conflicts I’ve had with students have been over phones. I’ve had students storm out of my room and even cuss me out when I’ve demanded they hand over their phones. This kind of power struggle creates more problems than it solves.

Second, and more important, teachers should not have to police smartphone usage in the first place. Smartphones and social media apps are designed to capture users’ attention. What’s been bothering me lately is that everyone knows this, yet as a society, we willingly allow — or more accurately, encourage — high school students to bring what are perhaps the greatest tools of distraction ever created onto school campuses every single day. Consider, for a moment, all the actions and decisions that lead the average student to show up to class with a phone in their pocket: the billions invested in software and hardware development; the research devoted to figuring out how to keep users engaged; the choice by individual parents to allow their child to have a phone. All these factors implicitly encourage students to bring their phones with them to school. And yet individual teachers are somehow supposed to prevent teenagers from using these devices? It’s absurd.

Instead of placing the burden on individual teachers, we need to shift the culture of schools to the point where having a smartphone in a classroom would be akin to smoking in one; it just wouldn’t happen. The best way to initiate this shift would be for the California Department of Education to issue policy guidance stating that phones should not be accessible to students during the academic school day — in other words, ban phones from classrooms. If students need to bring a phone to school, they can check it in with the front office before first period (phone lockers are a thing). This would take the onus off teachers and administrators to enforce individualized prohibitions. Instead, state policy would do the work of telling students and parents, “No.”

I am aware that there are drawbacks to an outright ban, not the least of which is that it removes structured, supervised opportunities for teenagers to learn how to develop healthy relationships with their phones. Screens and mobile devices are our lived reality, and there’s a legitimate argument that we have a responsibility to teach students how to resist the endless drip of notifications and the pull of digital pacification. There are also passing moments when smartphones can be useful during a lesson, through apps like Kahoot and Poll Everywhere. Finally, some parents might worry that a ban will make it difficult to reach their child during an emergency.

While I’m sensitive to these arguments, as a constant witness to how phones destroy student attention, they ultimately don’t hold up. Every main office and classroom I know of still has a landline for emergency contact. And more critically, as educators, we have to create environments that are suitable for learning and are in students’ best interests. Smartphones undermine both of those goals, to say nothing of their dangers to teen mental health. If we genuinely want what is best for students, California should take the lead on getting phones out of K-12 classrooms.

•••

Heath Madom teaches humanities at Life Academy of Health and Bioscience in the Oakland Unified School District. After a short-lived experiment with a dumbphone, he is currently trying to curb his own smartphone usage with the help of a kSafe.

    Having been a middle school science teacher (now retired) I can fully understand the desire to create a ban. I took a different direction. I created a detailed contract for the use of what was initially a cell phone but grew to include smartphones, tablets and wearable devices (Apple Watch).     Banning cell phones in schools is unrealistic and unsafe, as anyone who has lived through an on campus incident can attest. I vote for making cellphone lockers within classrooms a common practice. Students can park their phones in the locker on their way in and pick them up on their way out. The lockers illuminate to show which drawers are occupied/unoccupied. Students are issued a unique locker code that works on any locker in the school. Students would be able to access their phones for class activities or in the event of an emergency as needed. We need to update classroom management systems and practices to meet the changing times.
